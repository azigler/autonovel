# THE APPETITE REMAINING
## Chapter Outline — 11 Chapters, 55–65k words

---

## FORESHADOWING THREADS

**1. The Threat Assessment Habit**
Planted in Chapter 1 — Astarion catalogues every room he enters: exits, threats, useful objects, who could be bribed. By Chapter 6, the catalogue stalls mid-room and he can't remember why he started it. By Chapter 9, he catches himself cataloguing the bakery and the list becomes: the crack in the second shelf, the way Marta's hands move when she's counting change, the smell of the bread before the door opens. The habit doesn't disappear. It changes what it's measuring.

**2. The Crumbs**
First crumb appears in Chapter 2, accidental — he drops something, doesn't clean it up, tells himself he'll do it later. The rat appears in Chapter 3 without comment. By Chapter 5 he is "imprecise" about crumbs near the skirting board, and the prose does not remark on this. Chapter 6: the rat doesn't come. Chapter 7: the rat returns and he is standing very still when it does. Chapter 10: he leaves a deliberate crumb and the prose notes this without judgment. The word "deliberate" does the work.

**3. The East-Facing Bench**
Introduced in Chapter 3 as a practical observation — the stone is warm because the bench faces east and catches afternoon light. He sits because his legs are tired, not because he wants to. By Chapter 5 he notes the bench before he notes anything else about the square. Chapter 8: he arrives and someone else is sitting on it and he has to stand near the fountain instead, and the chapter does not say he minds, but he stands facing east. Chapter 10: he is on the bench when the letter arrives.

**4. The Word "Choose"**
The first sentence uses "chosen." The word recurs at precise intervals — always slightly wrong, always in contexts where choice is ambiguous or performed rather than genuine. Chapter 4: Karlach's letter uses it three times in the way only Karlach uses words, with full belief in their meaning. Chapter 6: in first person, he writes the word and then cannot finish the sentence. Chapter 11: the word appears once, in the last paragraph, and by then the reader knows what it costs him to mean it.

**5. The Letter from Cazador's Estate**
Planted as absence — in Chapter 2, a solicitor's office is mentioned in passing, the kind of place that handles the dissolution of criminal estates. In Chapter 5, he wonders briefly and dismissively what happens to a vampire lord's property when the vampire lord is ash. Chapter 8, a neighbor mentions receiving a letter from a firm she doesn't recognize. Chapter 10: the letter arrives. The reader has been waiting for it longer than Astarion has.

**6. The Question of Hunger**
Chapter 1 establishes that he has always had to manage hunger as performance — appear satisfied, appear in control, appear as if the hunger is not the only thing that is real. In Chapter 4 he notices he is hungry in a way that is not about blood — something shapeless and harder to name. Chapter 7, he tries to catalogue what he wants and the list is absurd and true: warmth, the specific quality of afternoon light, the sound of the rat moving behind the wall. Chapter 9: Marta gives him the bread and he holds it on the walk home, not eating it, just warm in his hands. Chapter 11: he eats the bread at the bench. He chose it.

**7. The Scar on His Wrist**
Cazador's mark — barely mentioned in Chapter 1, noted clinically as if it belongs to someone else's body. Chapter 3, he catches himself covering it in a social situation out of reflex, then stops, then covers it anyway because he wants to, not because he has to. Chapter 6, in first person, he looks at it and the prose looks with him, without flinching, without resolution. Chapter 9, a neighbor's child asks about it and he gives an answer that is true and brief and he is surprised to find this is enough. Chapter 11: the letter is from Cazador's estate. He reads it with his wrist visible on the table.

## RECURRING ELEMENTS

**The Bread** — appears first as something he accepts because refusing would be conspicuous. Becomes the thing he holds on the walk home. Becomes the thing he eats on the bench at the end. The bread is the same bread. He is not the same person holding it.

**The Catalogue** — threat assessment habit. Begins as armor, becomes a way of noticing, ends as a way of caring. What he catalogues is what he values. The reader can track his transformation through what makes the list.

**The Season's Light** — each chapter is anchored to what the light is doing. Not described for beauty but for information: this is what the light tells you about where you are in the year. Astarion reads light the way a sailor reads weather. By the end, he reads it the way a person reads something they love.

**"He didn't—"** — a syntactic tic in the prose. "He didn't move." "He didn't say anything." "He didn't clean it up." These negatives track what he refrains from doing as precisely as what he does. Freedom is first visible in what he stops performing.

**The Rat** — never named, never called a pet, never fed deliberately. Its presence is registered in small physical details: the sound behind the wall, the evidence of crumbs disappearing, once a glimpse of grey at the edge of his vision. Its absence in Chapter 6 is the emotional center of the novel.

**Warmth** — temperature as emotional cartography. He is cold. The bench is warm. Marta's bread is warm. Shadowheart is cool and precise and somehow comfortable. Karlach's letter is warm in the way Karlach is warm: it takes up more space than it should. By the end, he has arranged his days around sources of warmth so gradually he cannot say when it started.

### CHAPTER 1: *The First Choice*
**SEASON:** Late summer. The city is thick and slow with heat that he feels as information rather than discomfort.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 4,500–5,500

**BEAT:** The morning after the Netherbrain falls — or rather, many mornings after, compressed into one: Astarion alone in a rented room in the Lower City, facing the first day with no task assigned to him. He goes to a market. He stands in front of a vendor's stall for an unreasonable amount of time. He buys something. He doesn't eat it.

**UNDERNEATH:** The performance of freedom is immediately revealed as performance. He expected liberation to feel like expansion — instead it feels like a room with all the furniture removed. He is very good at wanting things he cannot have. He has no practice at all with wanting things he can. The purchased item (a small cake, expensive, unnecessary) sits on his table like a question he doesn't know how to answer.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He stands in front of a market stall for four minutes without calculating whether the vendor is a threat, without positioning himself for an exit, without performing appetite for an audience. He stands there because he cannot decide. This is new.

**OBJECTS:**
- The rented room: a table, a chair, a window that faces west (wrong direction for a vampire, noted clinically)
- The cake, small, dusted with sugar, sitting on the table uneaten
- A list he has made of things he is supposed to want, written in his precise magistrate's hand
- The first sentence, given to the reader: *"He had been a vampire for two hundred years and had never once chosen what to eat for breakfast."*

### CHAPTER 2: *Inventory*
**SEASON:** Late summer becoming early autumn. The light is changing — he notices it like a threat, something shifting that he can't quite place.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 4,500–5,500

**BEAT:** Astarion furnishes the room. He approaches this as a tactical problem: what does a person who lives in a place require? He acquires objects with the thoroughness of someone who has never been allowed to own anything and the detachment of someone who doesn't yet believe the objects are his. He visits a solicitor's office about something unrelated — the dissolution of a criminal estate, paperwork, boring — and notices it only in passing.

**UNDERNEATH:** The furnishing is a performance for an audience of one. He is trying to construct the visible evidence of a life before the life itself exists. The objects are correct — a good chair, a decent rug, candles that smell of something other than tallow — but they sit in the room without settling, the way furniture sits in a house that hasn't been lived in yet. He catches himself arranging things for how they would look to a visitor. There is no visitor coming.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He buys a book he doesn't intend to read immediately. Not as a prop, not to display — he puts it face-down on the chair because he was in the middle of deciding something when he set it down, and he'll come back to it. This is the first thing in the room that is genuinely, uselessly his.

**OBJECTS:**
- A chair, good quality, that he sits in and then sits differently in, adjusting
- A rug, slightly wrong color, that he chose because the other one was already sold
- The book, face-down on the chair
- A first crumb, dropped near the skirting board, not cleaned up — the prose notes this without emphasis

### CHAPTER 3: *The Shape of an Afternoon*
**SEASON:** Early autumn. Leaves not yet turning but the light is doing something at the horizon that it wasn't doing before.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,000–6,000

**BEAT:** Astarion goes outside without a destination for the first time. He walks. He finds the bench — east-facing, stone, warm in the afternoon from a morning of sun. He sits. He observes the square with the threat-assessment habit running quietly in the background. The rat is seen for the first time, a grey shape at the edge of his vision back in the room, when he returns. He does not note this as significant.

**UNDERNEATH:** The afternoon is the first unstructured time he cannot fill with acquisition or performance. Walking without purpose makes him feel conspicuous in a way he can't justify — two hundred years of moving only when directed, and now direction must come from inside. The bench is warm. He sits on it longer than he intends to. This is not a decision. It is what happens when he stops making decisions.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He sits on a bench in a public square and does nothing for forty minutes. Not performing leisure. Not watching for threats that require action. Simply sitting because the stone is warm and he has nowhere to be. He will not be able to explain this later and won't try.

**OBJECTS:**
- The bench: east-facing, stone, the particular quality of warmth that comes from hours of absorbed sun
- A fountain nearby, its sound — water, pigeons, the ambient noise of a city that has survived and returned to itself
- His wrist, covered reflexively in passing, then uncovered — not a decision, just a hand moving differently
- The grey shape at the skirting board when he returns home, registered and filed under: not relevant

### CHAPTER 4: *Karlach's Letter*
**SEASON:** Autumn, proper. The trees in the square have turned. He notices this as he reads.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,500–6,500

**BEAT:** A letter arrives from Karlach — written from Avernus or from wherever she has landed, uncertain of her own address, the handwriting large and slightly uneven as if written in bad light or with a hand not used to stillness. The letter is long. It uses the word "choose" three times. It asks him how he is doing and means it with a specificity that is uniquely Karlach: not "are you well" but "are you eating enough, are you sleeping at all, are you being impossible to everyone or just yourself." Astarion reads it twice. He does not write back immediately.

**UNDERNEATH:** The letter arrives at the exact moment when the performance of freedom has begun to feel hollow enough that he can admit, privately, that something is wrong. Karlach's warmth is difficult to receive — it doesn't require anything of him except that he be real, and being real is the thing he is most out of practice with. The letter makes him aware of a hunger that has nothing to do with blood. He doesn't know what to call it. He files it under: not relevant.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He reads the letter twice. The second time is not for information. He will not examine this.

**OBJECTS:**
- The letter: multiple pages, Karlach's handwriting, a small smudge that might be engine-grease or ink or both
- The word "choose," appearing three times, meaning three different things
- The autumn trees visible through the window while he reads
- The unanswered letter on the table for the rest of the chapter, face-up

### CHAPTER 5: *The Bakery*
**SEASON:** Late autumn. Cold arriving. He notices it the way he notices all temperature: as information about his environment, not as something that happens to him.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,500–6,500

**BEAT:** He discovers the bakery by proximity — it is near the square with the bench, and the smell of bread at closing time is something his body registers before his mind does. He begins appearing at closing time, not regularly, not intentionally, but with a frequency that Marta (ordinary name, flour on her apron, no particular opinion of him) eventually responds to by saving him the end-of-day bread. She does this not out of affection but out of efficiency: he is always there, the bread would otherwise be waste. He accepts the bread. He is imprecise about crumbs near the skirting board when he gets home.

**UNDERNEATH:** He is building a routine without intending to. The bakery is not a choice — it is what happens when you go to the bench and the bench is near the bakery and the bakery smells like warmth and warmth is the thing his body moves toward without consulting him. He notices the bench before he notices anything else about the square. This should concern him more than it does.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He accepts something given to him — the bread, the small repeated gesture of it — without calculating what it costs the giver or what he is expected to return. Marta does not want anything from him. This takes him several visits to believe.

**OBJECTS:**
- The bakery: the smell of it before the door opens, the warmth that comes out when the door does
- Marta's hands, counting change, efficient, not particularly interested in him
- The bread, end-of-day, slightly past its best — he holds it on the walk home, warm in his hands
- The crumbs near the skirting board, imprecise, not cleaned up

### CHAPTER 6: *Three Days*
**SEASON:** Early winter. The first real cold. The bench is no longer warm.
**POV:** **FIRST PERSON** — the exact center of the novel, a threshold
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,000–6,000

**BEAT:** The rat doesn't come for three days. Astarion notices on the first day and does not note it. Notices on the second day and notes it, briefly, and dismisses it. On the third day, the catalogue stops. He is standing in the middle of the room, having begun a threat assessment of the space — exits, useful objects, who could be bribed — and realizes he cannot remember why he started it. He sits down. He does not go to the bench. He does not go to the bakery. He writes something. He does not finish it.

**UNDERNEATH:** The shift to first person enacts the thing the chapter is about: he can no longer observe himself from a comfortable distance. The rat's absence has exposed something he has been careful not to look at — that he has been making a life in the way water makes a path, not by deciding but by going where the ground slopes, and that this life now has things in it that can be absent. This is the threshold. On one side: freedom as performance, as acquisition, as the hedonism of someone who has been told to want things. On the other side: something that is actually his. He is standing in the doorway. He has not crossed it yet.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He sits with the unfinished sentence. He does not perform composure for himself. He does not make a list. He just sits there, in his room, in the cold, and the prose sits with him.

**OBJECTS:**
- The skirting board: the crumbs, undisturbed
- The unfinished sentence on paper — the word "choose" visible, the rest blank
- The room, which he has been in for months now and which has begun, without his permission, to feel like his
- The cold, which is just the cold, which has always been just the cold

### CHAPTER 7: *The Rat Returns*
**SEASON:** Mid-winter. Snow on the eaves. The bench is covered, he imagines, though he hasn't gone to check.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,000–6,000

**BEAT:** The rat returns on the fourth day. Astarion is standing very still when it does. He has been standing near the skirting board for an indeterminate amount of time, having told himself he was looking for something else. He does not move when the rat appears. The rat does not move either, for a moment. Then it takes the crumb and goes. Shadowheart visits for the first time — arrives unannounced, stays for an afternoon, drinks his wine, tells him very little about herself and observes him with the precision of someone who has been trained to read people. She does not mention the rat.

**UNDERNEATH:** The rat's return is not resolution — it is the thing that shows him that the three days were real, that the absence was real, that something in him was tracking it all along. Shadowheart's visit is the first time another person has been in the space he has made, and he finds himself cataloguing the room through her eyes — not performing it, actually seeing it. She is the first person who sees him here, in this life, in this room. She doesn't comment on what she sees. This is, somehow, the most generous thing she could do.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He tries to make a list of what he wants and does not censor the list when it becomes absurd. The list includes: warmth, the sound of the rat behind the wall, the specific light at the bench in October, the smell of bread before the door opens. He does not throw the list away.

**OBJECTS:**
- The rat: grey, small, taking the crumb — the prose describes this with the attention it deserves, which is enormous and which the prose must carry without announcing it
- The list, written, kept
- Shadowheart: her wine glass, her precise way of sitting, her silence which is not uncomfortable
- The room as seen, for the first time, from outside himself

### CHAPTER 8: *Neighbor*
**SEASON:** Deep winter becoming late winter. The cold is the same but the light is changing — longer by minutes, imperceptibly, but he is a creature who notices light.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,500–6,500

**BEAT:** A neighbor — Pell, a middle-aged cartographer with ink-stained hands and no interest in Astarion's history — begins a series of minor, uninvited interactions: a borrowed tool returned, a comment about the weather, an offered piece of information about a street that's been closed for repairs. Astarion responds to each of these with the baffled precision of someone who has never been on the receiving end of neighborliness and cannot determine what it costs or what it wants. Pell asks for nothing. Pell continues existing. The bench, Astarion discovers, is occupied by someone else one afternoon. He stands near the fountain instead, facing east.

**UNDERNEATH:** The accumulation of unchosen attachment is becoming visible to him and he doesn't have a framework for it. He was enslaved, then free, then performing freedom — but he skipped the part where you become embedded in a place, in its texture, in its small recurring human dramas. Pell represents the thing Astarion has no category for: a person who simply is near him, without agenda, without the transaction he's been trained to expect. The occupied bench is the first time he notices that he has preferences about his own life. He does not call this a preference. But he faces east.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He accepts a piece of neighborly information — the closed street, the alternate route — and says thank you and means it in a register that is not performance. The "thank you" is small and slightly awkward. This is what genuine gratitude sounds like when you're out of practice.

**OBJECTS:**
- Pell's ink-stained hands, taking a delivery with both hands, the gesture that means: I am receiving this seriously
- The fountain, and Astarion standing near it, facing east, while someone else sits on his bench
- A letter that Pell mentions receiving from an unfamiliar firm — planted, not examined
- The late-winter light, longer by minutes, which Astarion is tracking without knowing he's tracking it

### CHAPTER 9: *Shadowheart's Second Visit*
**SEASON:** Early spring. The trees in the square are doing something tentative. The bench is dry again.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 5,500–6,500

**BEAT:** Shadowheart returns, this time with slightly more warning — a note, a day's notice, the implication that she has been thinking about whether to come. She is different in a way that is hard to name: less defended, or differently defended, or perhaps simply more tired in a way that has made the defenses less necessary. They walk. They end up at the bench. Marta gives him the bread at closing and he holds it on the walk back, warm, and Shadowheart does not comment on this either. A neighbor's child asks about the scar on his wrist and he answers briefly and truthfully and is surprised to find this is enough.

**UNDERNEATH:** The chapter is about what accumulates when you aren't watching. Shadowheart sees a version of him that is different from the one she knew in the camp, and she does not say so, but he can tell she sees it. He can tell because he sees the change in her, too, and says nothing. This is what friendship looks like when it isn't performing itself: two people sitting on a bench in early spring, noticing each other, saying very little. The bread is warm in his hands. He holds it the whole walk home. He eats it at the bench, finally — not in this chapter, but almost. Almost.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He answers the child's question about the scar truthfully and briefly — "an old injury, a long time ago" — without covering it, without performing nonchalance, without the elaborate deflection he would have reached for in Chapter 3. The answer is true. It is enough. He moves on.

**OBJECTS:**
- The bench in early spring, warm again — he notes this before anything else
- The bread, warm in his hands on the walk home
- Shadowheart's note (a day's advance warning — the prose notes this is an apology and a reaching-out, neither of which she would name)
- The child's question, brief, innocent, the scar visible in the spring light

### CHAPTER 10: *The Letter*
**SEASON:** Late spring. The square is in full leaf. The bench is warm every afternoon.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 6,000–7,000

**BEAT:** The letter from Cazador's estate arrives. It is from a solicitor's firm — the same one mentioned in passing in Chapter 2 — and it concerns a minor administrative matter: a property claim, a small sum held in escrow, something that requires his signature or his explicit refusal. It is bureaucratic. It is mundane. He is on the bench when it reaches him, delivered by a city messenger who does not know what it is. He reads it on the bench. He leaves a deliberate crumb when he gets home.

**UNDERNEATH:** The letter is not the crisis he has been bracing for — there is no crisis. The estate is ash, the power is gone, the long arm of the thing that owned him has been reduced to a solicitor's letter about escrow. He expected to feel something large. He feels something small and specific: the letter is an administrative artifact of a life that is over, and he is sitting on a warm bench in late spring, and the rat will be waiting, and Marta will save him the bread tomorrow, and Pell will nod at him in the corridor. The deliberate crumb is the chapter's center of gravity. The prose does not explain it.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He leaves a deliberate crumb. The word "deliberate" is the most important word in the chapter. He knows he is doing it. He does it anyway.

**OBJECTS:**
- The letter: solicitor's letterhead, his name in formal script, the word "escrow"
- The bench, warm, in full-leaf spring
- The deliberate crumb, placed near the skirting board
- His wrist, visible, on the table while he reads — the scar in the late spring light, noted once, not dwelt upon

### CHAPTER 11: *Breakfast*
**SEASON:** Early summer. A year completed. The light is the light of the beginning, but everything it falls on is different.
**POV:** Third person
**WORD COUNT TARGET:** 6,500–7,500

**BEAT:** Astarion writes back to Karlach. He goes to the bakery. He eats the bread — not at home, not standing up, not as an afterthought — he eats it on the bench, in the morning, in the early summer light. He has signed the solicitor's letter and sent it back, choosing one of the two options, and the prose tells us which without explaining why. He goes home. The rat is there. The chapter ends before anything else happens.

**UNDERNEATH:** The chapter is not an ending. It is the first morning of something that has been becoming itself for a year. The freedom he has arrived at is nothing like the freedom he imagined — it is smaller, more specific, full of things he did not choose and cannot explain: a bench, a rat, a woman who saves him bread because he's always there. He has not healed. He has not resolved. He has simply become a person who goes to the bakery at closing and faces east when the bench is taken and leaves crumbs he knows will be found. This is the appetite remaining: not the hunger of the first chapter, performed and shapeless, but the hunger of a person who knows what he wants because he has been wanting it, quietly, for a year.

**THE THING HE COULDN'T DO BEFORE:** He eats the bread on the bench. He chose it. He means the word.

**OBJECTS:**
- Karlach's letter, replied to — the reply is not shown, but its existence is confirmed
- The bread, eaten on the bench, in the morning light
- The rat, present, behind the wall — its sound, registered, filed under: good
- The early summer light, which is the light of the first chapter but falls on a room that is lived in now, on a person who has been, quietly, practicing being free

## STRUCTURAL NOTES FOR THE WRITER

**The chapter 6 pivot:** The shift to first person should feel like a door opening into a smaller, more honest room. The prose that surrounds it — chapters 5 and 7 — should be the closest to first person without being it, so the reader feels the pressure building before the shift and the release after. The return to third person in chapter 7 should feel like surfacing, not retreat.

**The rat as structural device:** The rat appears in chapters 3, 5, 6 (absent), 7, 8, 9, 10, and 11. It is never named. The prose never says Astarion cares about it. But the rat's appearances mark every chapter in the second half of the novel, and its presence at the end — "its sound, registered, filed under: good" — is the closest thing the novel has to a declaration of love. This must be earned by the accumulation of every appearance before it.

**The bread as arc:** The bread is held (chapter 5), held longer (chapter 7), held the whole walk home (chapter 9), eaten on the bench (chapter 11). The arc of the bread is the arc of the novel. The reader should not notice until they look back.

**The word "deliberate":** Appears once in the outline (chapter 10, the crumb). It should appear only once in the novel. It is the hinge. Everything before it is Astarion doing things without fully knowing he's doing them. Everything after it is Astarion knowing. The word costs something. Use it once and mean it.

**The letter:** What he does with the solicitor's letter — which option he chooses, the small administrative act — should be mundane and should be shown, not explained. The reader should understand what it means without being told. If the prose explains it, the chapter fails. Trust the accumulation.
