He had been a vampire for two hundred years and had never once chosen what to eat for breakfast.

This was the thought that arrived before anything else — before the quality of the light through the window, before the sound of the street below, before the smell of the room, which was stale in the particular way of rooms that have hosted many strangers and absorbed something from all of them. The thought arrived fully formed, as if it had been waiting. He lay on the rented bed and looked at the ceiling and let it sit there.

Two hundred years. Breakfast.

The ceiling had a crack in it that ran from the east wall to a point roughly above his left shoulder, where it branched into two smaller cracks and stopped. Someone had attempted to plaster over the original damage at some point; he could see the different texture of it in the morning light, a slightly raised ridge that didn't match the rest. A repair that hadn't quite taken. The room was otherwise unremarkable — a bed, a chest of drawers missing its bottom pull, a window with a shutter that didn't close all the way, a small table near the door. He had catalogued it last night upon arrival, standing in the doorway for a moment before entering: exits (one, the door; one, the window, second story, manageable), sight lines (window faces east, observable from the building across the street, distance approximately forty feet), potential threats (none immediate; the innkeeper was tired and incurious; the other guests, heard through the wall, were arguing about money in the low exhausted way of people who had been arguing about the same money for a long time).

He had slept anyway. Which was new.

Not sleep itself — he had slept, or something like it, in the coffin. But that had been enforced stillness, the body doing what it was told, and whatever rest it provided had the quality of a closed fist rather than an open hand. Last night he had lain down on a rented mattress that smelled of other people's dreams and he had simply — stopped. No instruction. No permission. He had woken up with his face turned toward the window and the early light coming through the gap in the shutter, and there had been a moment, a handful of seconds, before the cataloguing started.

Just him and the ceiling crack and the silence.

Then: exits, sight lines, threats. The habit was faster than the thought. He had noted, in rapid succession, that the crack had not changed overnight, that the light suggested early morning, mid-summer, perhaps six o'clock or just past it, that the arguing neighbors had either resolved their dispute or run out of energy for it, that he was alone in the room, that the door was still latched, that his boots were where he had left them.

And then, rising from somewhere underneath all of that: two hundred years. Breakfast.

He sat up.

The floorboards were cool under his feet. He had boots — good ones, taken from a man who had not needed them anymore — and a change of clothes in the chest of drawers, and approximately forty gold pieces in the inner pocket of his coat, and no one in the world who required anything of him at this particular moment. He sat with this for a second. The room was quiet. From the street below came the sound of a cart wheel on cobblestone, someone calling a name, the distant clatter of a market setting up.

He was free.

He waited for it to feel like something.

---

The market was three streets west and one north, which he had identified last night on the walk from the docks. He found it the same way he found everything — by cataloguing the route as he walked it, storing intersections and landmarks with the automatic efficiency of two centuries of necessary caution. Left at the apothecary with the cracked sign. Straight past the tailor's. Right where the street narrowed and the buildings leaned toward each other overhead like conspirators.

The market in the morning was different from the market he had passed last night. Last night it had been a few stubborn stalls and the smell of things that hadn't sold. Now it was noise and motion and the specific chaos of commerce: vendors calling, children running, a woman arguing with a fishmonger in a way that suggested the argument was recreational. He stopped at the entrance and looked.

It was a reasonable size. Maybe thirty stalls. He could see, from here: dried herbs in hanging bundles, the smell of them reaching even across the crowd (thyme, something floral, something he didn't recognize). A stall with bolts of cloth. Another with what appeared to be second-hand tools. Three food stalls visible from this angle — bread, he could smell that too, and something frying in oil, and the sharp green smell of vegetables that had been cut recently.

He was cataloguing.

He knew he was cataloguing. He did it anyway, because the alternative was standing at the entrance to a market in the early morning not knowing what to do next, and that was not a situation he intended to inhabit for long.

The bread stall first, he decided. Not because he had decided on bread, exactly, but because it was the nearest food stall and the crowd around it was sparse enough that he could approach without being jostled. He was not, at present, in the mood to be jostled. He was barely in the mood to be here at all, and he recognized this as a problem, because here was where breakfast lived, and breakfast was apparently the first thing freedom required of him.

The woman behind the bread stall was perhaps fifty, with the kind of face that had spent a long time in weather and arrived at something weathered and particular. She was arranging loaves on the counter with the practiced economy of someone who had done this ten thousand times and stopped needing to think about it. She glanced at him when he stopped in front of her stall, made some rapid assessment, and returned to her arrangement.

"Morning," she said. Not warmly. Not unwarmly. A transaction beginning.

"Good morning," he said, and heard himself say it with the automatic polish that two hundred years of Cazador's household had installed in him, the particular tone that said I am worth speaking to and you should feel fortunate that I'm speaking to you. It came out before he could decide whether he wanted it to. He looked at the bread.

There were seven varieties. He had not expected seven varieties. He had expected bread — the category — and instead there were seven instances of it, each subtly different, and the woman was waiting for him to choose, and he had no framework for choosing because for two hundred years the framework had been: what is available, what can be acquired without detection, what will Cazador's household accept, what is the least that can be eaten without drawing attention.

None of those frameworks applied here.

"What's the difference," he said, and then, because that had come out sounding more genuine than intended, added, "between these two," and pointed to the nearest loaves. A recovery. Partial.

The woman looked at him with the expression of someone who had answered this question many times and had opinions about people who asked it. "That one's got rosemary. That one doesn't."

"Right." He looked at the rosemary loaf. It was a small loaf, round, the top scored in a crosshatch pattern. It smelled — he tried to identify what it smelled like. Warm, obviously. Something underneath the rosemary that was just bread, yeast and flour and the particular smell of baking, which was a smell he had encountered in two hundred years but never quite aimed at himself. "I'll take the rosemary one."

She named a price. He paid it. She handed him the loaf in a square of cloth and he took it and stepped away from the stall and stood in the market with a small round loaf of bread in his hands and no instructions for what to do next.

He ate a piece of it standing there.

It was — fine. The rosemary was present but not aggressive. The bread itself had a good crust, a soft interior, still warm from the oven. He chewed it carefully, which was a habit from the years when eating anything other than blood had required a performance of normality — look like someone eating, look like someone who has always eaten, look like nothing is wrong. The habit was still there. He was performing eating for an audience of thirty strangers who were not looking at him.

He noticed this and kept chewing anyway.

The bread tasted like the absence of instructions. That was the most accurate thing he could say about it. Not bad. Not particularly transcendent. Just — food, chosen by him, for no reason except that he'd been standing in front of it and it had smelled warm. The hunger in him was the ordinary kind, the kind that asked for something and then quieted when it received it. He had been expecting something more complicated from it.

A woman walked past him with a basket over one arm and a child attached to the other. The child was explaining something at length and with great conviction; the woman was listening with the expression of someone who was mostly thinking about something else. He watched them pass and then looked down at the loaf in his hands.

He had eaten perhaps a quarter of it. He was still holding it. He was standing in the middle of a market in Baldur's Gate at six o'clock in the morning having chosen, of his own volition, to be here and to eat this bread, and the freedom of this was — he turned the thought over. The freedom of this was present. He could feel it the way you feel a room that has been cold for a long time — not warmth exactly, but the absence of one particular cold. He had expected something more dramatic. Something that felt like the sun on his face that morning in camp, the clean shock of it, the recognition. Instead this felt like: he was in a market. He had bread. The morning was doing what mornings did.

He walked further into the market.

---

The cataloguing continued because it always continued. He was aware of this about himself — aware in the way you are aware of a habit that has outlived its original purpose but hasn't found a new one yet. He catalogued the market stalls (cloth, tools, bread, fish, vegetables, a peculiar stall selling what appeared to be both birdcages and second-hand shoes, a woman selling remedies from a cart that smelled strongly of alcohol and false promise). He catalogued the people (the fishmonger, male, large, arguing with the woman who had moved on to a second argument about a different fish; a young man at the tool stall who had the look of someone sent to buy something specific and unable to find it; several people he couldn't immediately categorize, which meant they went into the watch-for column by default). He catalogued the exits from the market square (three, plus two alleys that might become exits under duress). He catalogued the sight lines, the shadows, the places a person might stand to observe without being observed.

He was doing it before he'd decided to. He was always doing it before he'd decided to.

It occurred to him, somewhere between the vegetable stall and the peculiar birdcage-and-shoes establishment, that this was not really observation. Observation implied looking at things to learn from them, to appreciate them, to understand. What he was doing was something else — a constant low-level accounting of the world, a list he kept not to refer back to but simply to keep. As if the list itself were the point. As if, while the list was running, something else couldn't get in.

He stopped in front of the birdcage stall.

The cages were empty, most of them. A few had birds in — small brown things that moved from perch to perch with the mechanical patience of creatures that had made peace with enclosure. The shoes were arranged on a table beside the cages in no particular order, mismatched pairs mixed with matched ones, the kind of collection that accrued when someone bought job lots at auction and sorted them later. Or never.

"Looking for a cage or a shoe?" the stallkeeper asked. He was a small man with a very large moustache and the air of someone who had heard every joke about his combined inventory and had transcended them.

"Neither, at the moment." He looked at the nearest cage — small, brass, a hook at the top for hanging. The bird inside it was sitting very still, watching him with one eye. "How does one end up selling both?"

"My wife kept birds," the man said. "My brother-in-law kept shoes. They both died in the same winter and I inherited the stock." He said this without particular grief, in the tone of someone who had explained it many times and arrived at a settled relationship with the facts. "You'd be surprised how often people need one or the other."

"I imagine I would be." He looked at the bird. The bird looked back. "Do they mind? The birds."

The man considered this with apparent sincerity. "Hard to say. They eat, they sing sometimes. They seem all right." He paused. "They don't have a lot of opinions about it one way or the other, far as I can tell."

He thought about this for a moment. Then he thanked the man and moved on.

The vegetable stall was next. He bought — he was not sure why he bought them — two late-summer tomatoes, very red, still warm from sitting in the morning sun. They went into his coat pocket alongside the remainder of the bread. He was accumulating breakfast, he realized. Not planning it, just accumulating it, picking up things that presented themselves. This was probably how most people did it. He had no way to know.

Past the vegetable stall the market thinned out, the stalls giving way to the edge of the square and then to a side street that ran south toward the harbor. He could smell the harbor from here, salt and fish and the particular smell of large amounts of water. He had arrived by harbor. He did not have strong feelings about the harbor. He stood at the edge of the market and ate one of the tomatoes and thought about nothing in particular, which was harder than it sounded.

The tomato was very good. That surprised him slightly. He had been eating tomatoes, when he ate tomatoes, for two hundred years, and they had always been — fine. A thing to eat. This one tasted like it had been grown in actual sunlight by someone who cared whether it was good, and it was still warm from that sun, and he stood at the edge of a market in Baldur's Gate and ate it and felt something that was not quite gratitude and not quite grief and didn't have a name he wanted to put on it.

He ate the second one too.

---

He walked for a while after that. Not toward anything. The city was waking up around him — the streets filling, the noise building, the particular rhythm of a large city in the morning establishing itself. He had been in Baldur's Gate before, briefly, in the years immediately after Cazador had taken him, when the household had moved through the city on whatever business the household had. He had not paid much attention then. Paying attention to things outside the immediate radius of survival had not been a priority.

He paid attention now.

The streets in this part of the city were old — the buildings had the look of things that had been built in one century and modified in several subsequent ones, additions and repairs in different styles layered on top of each other like geological strata. There was a house on the corner of one street that had a wooden upper story built onto a stone lower story, the wood newer by perhaps fifty years, and someone had at some point painted the wood a dark red that had faded to a color that was more suggestion than pigment. Next to it a building that was entirely stone, four stories, with window boxes on the third floor that were mostly dead vegetation and one unexpected burst of yellow flowers.

He looked at the yellow flowers for a moment.

They were ordinary flowers. He didn't know what kind. They were very yellow against the grey stone, and someone had planted them and someone had watered them and they were still alive in the late summer heat, and this was not remarkable in any way, and he stood on the street and looked at them anyway.

The cataloguing, when it came back — and it came back, it always came back — sorted the flowers into: non-threatening, ornamental, indicative of habitation above (someone lives there, tends things, has preferences about color). He watched himself do this and found it faintly absurd. The flowers were yellow. He could just — let them be yellow.

He kept walking.

The street he was on ran gradually uphill and then crested into a small square with a fountain that was not working — the basin full of still water and a summer's worth of debris — and a bench along the east side that caught the morning sun. He sat on the bench. He was not tired. He sat anyway, because the bench was there and the sun was on it and he had nowhere to be.

This was the thing he kept running into. He had nowhere to be.

Two hundred years of having somewhere to be — of the day structured by Cazador's requirements, the night structured by Cazador's commands, the whole of his existence organized around someone else's schedule and someone else's needs — and now the day was entirely empty of structure, and he was sitting on a bench in a square with a broken fountain and a bread loaf in his coat and nowhere to be, and it felt —

Not bad. He checked. It felt not bad. But it felt like standing in a room where the furniture had been removed. The room was fine. The room was his. The emptiness of it was just — present.

He broke off another piece of the bread and ate it.

A cat appeared from somewhere and sat at a careful distance from the bench, watching him with the professional suspicion of a city cat that had learned not to expect much from strangers. He looked at the cat. The cat looked at him. He broke off a small piece of bread and held it out, not particularly hopefully.

The cat considered this for a long moment and then walked away.

"Fair enough," he said, to the square, to the non-working fountain, to no one in particular.

---

He spent an hour in the city. He knew it was approximately an hour because the sun moved, and he had spent enough time tracking the sun — first in its absence, then in its sudden return — to have a reasonably accurate sense of it. He walked south toward the harbor and then north again through a different set of streets. He found a district that appeared to be primarily inhabited by people who made things — he could hear hammering from at least two directions, and one building had its ground floor open to the street and a man inside it bent over a workbench, doing something with leather. He paused to watch. The man did not look up. The work was its own world.

He found, a street further on, a small square where three old men were playing a card game at a table someone had dragged outside. They were playing with the focused silence of people who had been playing together long enough that commentary was unnecessary. He watched them for a moment from the edge of the square. The game appeared to involve a good deal of slapping cards on the table with more force than was strictly required. One of the men had a cup of something steaming at his elbow; he picked it up periodically and drank from it without looking away from the cards.

He walked on.

He found a bookstall tucked into an alley between a bakery and what he thought might be a locksmith. The books were arranged on a table with the cheerful disorder of a collection that had been organized once and then abandoned to entropy. He stopped and looked at them. The seller — a young woman reading her own book behind the table — glanced up, assessed him, and returned to her reading. He appreciated this. He picked up a book from the nearest pile.

It was a history. Old — the binding was worn to something softer than leather, the pages the color of old cream. He opened it to a random page and read a paragraph about a siege that had happened in a city he had never been to, two centuries before he had been born. The prose was dry and confident in the way of histories that had decided what mattered and were not taking further questions.

He read the paragraph again.

He had not read for pleasure — not really, not in the way of choosing to read rather than reading because it was required — in a very long time. He had read Cazador's correspondence, when Cazador wanted correspondence read. He had read ledgers, occasionally, when ledgers were what was needed. He had read whatever was put in front of him and retained it because retaining things was useful and being useful had been the entire architecture of his survival.

He turned to the front of the book. The title page said it was a history of the Sword Coast in the third century, and below that, in a different hand, someone had written a name — previous owner, he assumed, the way books accumulated names — and below that, in a third hand, a date that was sixty years ago.

He bought it.

The young woman named a price without looking up from her own book. He paid it. She took the coin and tucked it somewhere without comment. He stood in the alley with the history of the Sword Coast under his arm and felt something that might have been — he examined it carefully. Satisfaction, maybe. The specific satisfaction of acquiring something for no reason except wanting it, which was a satisfaction he had not had much occasion to feel.

He put the book under his arm and walked back toward the inn.

---

The room was as he had left it. He had known it would be, because he had locked the door and checked the window latch before leaving, but knowing and arriving to find it true were two slightly different things. He stood in the doorway for a moment and catalogued it again: the crack in the ceiling, the chest of drawers with the missing pull, the window with the gap in the shutter. The light had changed — it was past seven now, maybe closer to eight, and the sun had moved far enough that it came through the gap at a different angle, a bar of light across the floorboards rather than the wall.

He sat on the edge of the bed and put the book on the table. He took the remaining bread out of his coat and set it on the table beside the book. He looked at these two things — the history of the Sword Coast, the mostly-eaten rosemary loaf — and thought that they were both things he had chosen today, which was more things than he had chosen in any given day for the last two hundred years.

He had been expecting this thought to feel larger than it did.

He opened the book to the first page and began to read.

The prose was, as promised, dry. The historian had opinions about the relative importance of trade routes versus military alliances in the development of the coastal cities, and he was not shy about sharing them. Astarion read three pages and found himself mildly interested in the trade routes question, which surprised him. He read another two pages. The historian was making a point about the relationship between the spice trade and the growth of Baldur's Gate specifically, and the point was not without merit, and he had not expected to find himself thinking this about a history book he had bought from a woman who hadn't looked up from her own reading, but here he was.

He set the book down after a while and looked at the ceiling crack.

The thing about the morning was that it had been — he tried to find the word for it. Ordinary. Not in a bad way. He had walked through a city in the morning and bought bread and tomatoes and a book and sat on a bench and watched three old men play cards, and none of it had required him to be anyone in particular or to perform anything for anyone, and no one had wanted anything from him, and the day had simply proceeded in the way days proceeded when nothing was trying to kill you and no one owned you and you had forty gold pieces and nowhere to be.

He had been expecting freedom to announce itself more clearly. He had been waiting, he realized, for a feeling that would confirm it — something like the sunrise that first morning in camp, the clean shock of recognition. Instead it kept arriving as small things. The tomato warm from the sun. The book that hadn't been his and now was. The bread that he'd chosen because it smelled like something and had turned out to be fine.

He was not sure what to do with small things. He had been very focused, for two hundred years, on large things. Survival. Cazador. The tadpole. The Netherbrain. Large things had a clarity to them — a clear problem, a clear response, a clear metric for whether you had succeeded. Small things were different. Small things just accumulated.

He picked up the book again.

On the floor near the window, he noticed, there were crumbs. He had moved through the room twice with the bread loaf in his coat, and apparently the coat pocket was not entirely secure, because there were seven crumbs — he counted them without deciding to count them — scattered across the floorboards near the window. Small, irregular. The kind of crumbs that resulted from a bread loaf carried in a pocket rather than a bag.

He looked at them.

He waited for the impulse to sweep them up, to correct the mess, to restore the room to its prior state. It was a rented room. He had a responsibility to the innkeeper's floors. He was not, as a rule, someone who left crumbs on other people's floors.

The impulse didn't arrive.

He looked at the crumbs for another moment and then looked back at the book. The historian had moved on to a discussion of the city's early fortifications and was being somewhat more interesting about it than expected. He read another page. The crumbs remained on the floor near the window, seven of them, in the bar of morning light.

He didn't know what that meant, exactly. He had a feeling it meant something. He filed it under things to consider later and kept reading.

---

Sometime after nine the noise from the street changed character — the market settling into its midday rhythm, the particular quality of a city that had been awake for several hours and was no longer surprised by itself. He had read forty pages of the history of the Sword Coast. He knew more than he had previously known about the spice trade and was forming views about it.

He set the book down and lay back on the bed and looked at the ceiling crack.

The thing he hadn't told anyone — hadn't had occasion to tell anyone, because there hadn't been anyone, because he had made that choice too, the choice to be here alone rather than elsewhere with people, which was its own kind of choice — was that the anticipation of freedom had been so much cleaner than the thing itself. He had known, in the abstract, that freedom would be complicated. He had been a magistrate once, before Cazador, and he had known something about the difference between what laws said and what people did with them. He had not been naive. But he had thought — he had thought it would feel more like arrival.

Instead it felt like the first morning in a new city. The city was fine. The city had bread and tomatoes and books and old men playing cards. The city was full of things that could be looked at and bought and eaten and thought about. He was in it, and he was free, and the freedom was real — he could feel its reality in the absence of the other thing, the constant pressure of Cazador's will, the way the air had always felt slightly compressed before. That was gone. He could feel it being gone.

The trouble was that gone was a different thing from replaced. The space where the pressure had been was just space. He didn't know yet what to put in it.

He supposed that was the work. He supposed that was what the next however-many-years were — finding out what you put in the space. He had the bread and the tomatoes and the book and the crumbs on the floor, and he had forty gold pieces and a rented room and a city he had barely started to learn, and he had, apparently, opinions about the spice trade, which was not something he had expected to have by nine o'clock in the morning.

It was not nothing.

He looked at the ceiling crack. The plaster repair that hadn't quite taken, the ridge where someone had tried to fix something and fixed it imperfectly and then presumably moved on, because the ceiling was still standing and the crack was not getting worse. He had been in this room for less than a day. He would be in it for — he hadn't decided. A week, maybe. Until he had a better sense of the city, until he had made whatever decisions came next.

He could stay as long as he needed to.

He turned this over. He could stay as long as he needed to, because no one was going to tell him to leave, because no one had any authority over when he left, because he was the only person in the world with a stake in the question of how long Astarion Ancunín stayed in a rented room in Baldur's Gate. This was either liberating or alarming, and he had not yet determined which, and possibly it was both, and possibly it would be both for some time.

He picked up the book.

The crumbs stayed where they were on the floor near the window, seven of them, in the light.

He read until the light changed again.
