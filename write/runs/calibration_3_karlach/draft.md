The forge was louder than she remembered.

Karlach stood in the doorway for a moment, not quite entering, letting the sound hit her — the hammer's rhythm, the hiss of quenched metal, the low constant roar of the fire that made everything else a kind of vibration more than noise. She'd walked past this door six times in the last week. Maybe seven. She'd been counting other things and lost track of this one.

She went in.

Dammon had his back to her. He was working on something at the main anvil, something small enough that he'd brought a different hammer — lighter, shorter-handled — and the blows landed with a precision that was nothing like the broad strokes she'd watched him use on the infernal iron. He didn't look up. Either he hadn't heard her over the forge, or he was the kind of man who finished a thought before he started a new one. She'd always suspected the latter.

She didn't announce herself. She found a spot near the wall, far enough from the fire to be out of his way, and stood.

The forge smelled like scorched iron and oil and something underneath both of those things, something mineral and hot that she'd never been able to name. She'd grown up in the city, before, and then spent ten years in Avernus where everything smelled like sulfur and blood and the specific acrid note of demon-flesh when it burned. This was nothing like that. This was just — heat. The kind that came from a thing doing the work it was built to do.

She breathed it in and felt something in her shoulders drop that she hadn't known was held.

Dammon set the light hammer down and picked up a pair of tongs. Whatever he was working on glowed a deep orange at its edges, not quite red, and he turned it once in the firelight and looked at it the way she'd seen surgeons look at wounds — not alarmed, not emotional, just assessing. He set it back in the coals.

"Karlach," he said, without turning around.

"Dammon."

"Give me ten minutes."

"Take twenty," she said. "I'm not here for anything."

He did turn then, just enough to look at her over his shoulder. His face was sheened with sweat and the firelight caught the burn scars along his jaw in a way that made them look almost decorative, though she knew better than to say so. He had the expression of a man who had heard that claim before and was reserving judgment.

"Alright," he said, and turned back to the fire.

She watched him work.

---

The ten minutes turned into something longer. She didn't mind. The forge was loud enough that she didn't have to fill the silence with anything — there was no silence to fill, just the hammer and the fire and the occasional sharp hiss of steam when he quenched. She could stand here and not perform. That was rarer than it sounded.

People expected things from her. She knew it and she didn't resent it exactly, but she was aware of it the way you're aware of a pack that's been fitted to your frame — you stop noticing the weight until you put it down, and then you notice all at once. Wyll wanted her to be brave about it, because he needed someone to be brave about something. Shadowheart wanted her to be honest, which was its own kind of pressure. Astarion wanted her to be cheerful, which was the version of her he'd built his affection around and she wasn't sure what happened to that affection if she stopped being it.

Dammon wanted nothing from her. He was making something and she was watching and the forge was very loud.

She looked at his hands.

She'd noticed them before — you couldn't not, if you paid attention to people, and she'd always paid attention to people. It was what kept you alive in Avernus. You learned to read everyone in a room in the first ten seconds, threat by threat, and the habit didn't leave just because the room was now a forge in Baldur's Gate and the man in it was making something rather than trying to kill her.

His hands were a record. Burns that had healed and burned again, layered over each other in striations of scar tissue that she recognized as years of work, not accident. Calluses on the pads of his fingers, the heel of his palm, the crook between thumb and forefinger where the hammer handle sat. He held the tongs with a sureness that was almost casual, the kind of sureness that only comes from doing a thing so many times that your hands know it without consulting the rest of you.

She looked at her own hands.

They were big, bigger than most women's, which had always suited her fine. Good for fighting, good for lifting, good for the kind of work that needed force rather than finesse. But the engine — she'd stopped thinking of it as her heart a long time ago, the word was too small and too soft for what it was — the engine ran everything too hot now. She could feel it in her palms. A constant low-level warmth that on bad days climbed toward a burn.

She'd broken three cups since Baldur's Gate. Not from carelessness. From the heat transfer, which she couldn't always regulate, which sometimes spiked when she wasn't paying attention. The first cup she'd felt bad about. The second she'd replaced without saying anything. After the third she'd switched to metal ones and stopped apologizing.

Dammon had finished whatever he was doing with the tongs. He set the piece down on the anvil and came to the water barrel, rolling his sleeves back, and she watched him wash the sweat from his face and the back of his neck without any self-consciousness about being watched.

"You've been walking past," he said.

"You saw that."

"Forge faces the street." He dried his hands on a cloth that was more gray than white. "Saw you three times, maybe four. Figured you'd come in when you were ready."

She thought about denying it and decided not to bother. "Didn't want to interrupt."

"I'm always working," he said. "That's not an interruption, that's just timing." He moved back to the anvil, picked up the piece he'd been working on, turned it in his hand. It had cooled to a dull gray. She couldn't tell what it was yet. "You want tea? I've got a kettle."

"You have a kettle."

"Surprisingly civilized, I know."

She almost smiled. "Sure."

He hung the kettle over a lower hook, away from the main fire, where the heat was consistent but not scorching. She watched him do it with the same efficiency he brought to everything — no wasted motion, no fussing. He'd been working this forge long enough that the space had organized itself around his habits, the way a room always does for someone who's been in it long enough. Tools in reach. Water close. A stool positioned where the light was best.

She pulled a second stool from the corner and sat.

The forge was quieter with the hammer put down, but not quiet. The fire still roared. It was a sound like weather, she thought — you stopped hearing it after a while, it just became the texture of the air.

"How's the engine?" Dammon asked.

"Same."

He nodded, like that was the answer he'd expected. She appreciated that he didn't push. He'd seen enough of it to know what same meant — stable, which was not the same as good, and not the same as fixable. They'd established that. She'd made her peace with it, mostly, on most days.

"I'm not here about the engine," she said.

"I know." He picked up the piece again, examined it. "You said."

"I just wanted to—" She stopped. She'd had a sentence in her head for approximately six days and now that she was here it had gone somewhere she couldn't reach it. "I don't know. Walk past a forge and eventually you have to go in, right?"

"Is that how it works?"

"That's how it worked today."

He set the piece down again and gave her his full attention, which felt different than it did from most people. Most people gave you their attention and you could feel them building a response while you were still talking, feel the gears of their sympathy turning. Dammon just looked at her, and then he said: "The kettle's going to take a minute."

"That's fine."

"You can tell me what you're actually here about, or you can just sit. Either one is fine."

She looked at the fire. "I might just sit."

"Alright."

He picked up the piece and went back to work.

---

She sat for a while. The kettle began to make sounds that weren't quite boiling yet, a low murmur under the forge noise. Dammon had switched to a different tool — something small and pointed, a scribe of some kind — and was marking the piece with a precision that made her think of the surgeons again, the ones she'd watched work in field camps in Avernus, the ones who operated with that same quality of attention that was almost inhuman in its focus.

She thought about what she was afraid of.

She'd been thinking about it for six days, which was how long she'd been walking past the forge, and she hadn't gotten any closer to a clean answer. The engine ticking down — that was part of it. The cold that would follow. But underneath that, or maybe around it, there was something else she kept circling.

Ten years in Avernus, she'd known what she was. A weapon. A very good weapon, and she'd been proud of that in the way you could be proud of a thing when there was nothing else to be proud of. She'd fought because if she stopped fighting she died, and she'd made it mean something, made it into a choice even when it wasn't. She was Karlach. She killed things. She was very warm and very loud and she did not stop moving.

And then the tadpole, and the companions, and Baldur's Gate, and the brain.

And now.

She was still warm. Still loud, mostly. Still capable of killing things, though the city had fewer things that needed killing than Avernus had, which was probably good. But the urgency — the engine's urgency, the constant biological pressure of too much heat and not enough time — she'd been running on that for months. It had become the structure of her days. There was something to solve and she was solving it and every moment had a reason.

The brain was dead.

The engine was stable, which was not the same as fixed, which was not the same as forever.

She didn't know what she was without the urgency.

That was what she couldn't say to Wyll, because he needed her brave. Couldn't say to Shadowheart, who would try to help and whose helping would require Karlach to be articulate about something she wasn't articulate about yet. Couldn't say to Astarion, who had his own version of this problem and she wasn't sure he'd found an answer and she didn't want to make it worse.

The forge was very loud. She didn't have to say it here, either. She could just sit.

The kettle began to whistle.

Dammon set down the scribe and dealt with the kettle with the same efficiency he brought to everything, and when he handed her the cup it was a proper cup, not a camp mug, with a small chip on the rim that someone had sanded smooth so it wouldn't catch your lip. She held it with both hands and felt the warmth of it, which was a different warmth than the engine, which was a different warmth than the forge — a small, ordinary warmth, the kind that meant someone had thought about whether you were cold.

She wasn't cold. She was never cold. But she held the cup anyway.

"What are you making?" she asked.

He held up the piece. She could see now that it was a small handle — curved, the right size for a palm, with a slight flare at the base for grip. The marks he'd been scribing were along the edge, fine as embroidery.

"Don't know yet," he said. "Sometimes I start with a shape and find out what it is later."

"Does that work?"

"More often than you'd think." He set it on the bench beside him. "Sometimes it doesn't go anywhere. But the working is the thing, not always the result."

She looked at the handle. "You'd make a terrible merchant."

"I'm not a merchant." He picked up his tea. "I'm a smith."

"What's the difference?"

He thought about it. "A merchant starts with what someone wants and makes it. A smith starts with what the material wants to be." He turned the cup in his hands, not drinking yet. "Usually those align. Sometimes they don't."

"And when they don't?"

"You make a different thing than you planned." He shrugged. "Or you start over."

She thought about that. The engine ticked, low and steady, the way it always did when she was calm — she'd learned to hear it as a baseline, a pulse, the rhythm of herself. "What does it want to be?" she asked, nodding at the handle.

"Not sure yet. Could be a grip for a tool. Could be decorative. Could be neither." He picked it up again, turned it. "It's got a good weight to it, though. That much I know."

The fire shifted, a log settling, and the light changed for a moment — brighter, then steadying. Karlach watched the sparks rise and dissipate. She'd always liked that. The sparks going up and then gone, clean and purposeful and brief.

"Can I look at it?"

He handed it to her without hesitation.

She took it carefully — she always took things carefully now, calibrated her grip before she closed her fingers, checked the heat in her palms. The handle was cool, the metal having lost the forge's heat, and it sat in her hand with exactly the weight he'd described. Good weight. The kind that made your hand feel occupied rather than burdened.

The scribing along the edge was fine work. She tilted it toward the firelight and saw that it wasn't just marking — it was a pattern, something that repeated, though she couldn't quite resolve the whole of it from one end.

"What's the pattern?"

"Feathers," he said. "Or leaves. Depends on how you hold it."

She turned it and saw what he meant — the same line read differently depending on the angle, the marks branching in a way that could be either thing. "Hm."

"Hedging my bets," he said.

She handed it back. Her hand felt briefly empty after.

"Your hands," she said, and then stopped, because she hadn't planned to say that, it had just come out.

Dammon glanced at them, then back at her. "What about them?"

"The burns." She turned her own palm up. "You can still do that. The fine work."

"Takes longer than it used to." He flexed his right hand, a slow deliberate movement. "The scar tissue on the third finger doesn't have the same sensitivity. I have to compensate." He picked up the scribe, held it. "Press a little harder on the right side so I feel it evenly."

"But you can."

"I can." He set the scribe down. "Is that what you're thinking about?"

She looked at the fire. "I broke three cups."

He waited.

"Heat transfer. When I'm not paying attention, or when it spikes." She kept her eyes on the fire, which was easier than looking at him. "I switched to metal ones. But I keep thinking about — I used to make things. Before. Nothing like this, but. Small things. Carved things, sometimes, when we had downtime. You need a light touch for that."

"And now you don't have one."

"Now I have to think about it every time I pick something up." The engine ticked. "It's not — I'm not complaining. I know what I have. I know what I don't." She turned her palm over, closed her fingers. "I just think about it sometimes."

Dammon was quiet for a moment. The forge filled in the silence with its own voice, the way it always did.

"There are tools built for it," he said. "For working hot. Gloves, handles, grip modifications. You could still carve, if you wanted. It would just need different equipment."

"I know."

"But that's not really what you're talking about."

She looked at him then. He was watching her with that same quality of attention — not building a response, just taking in what was there. It was restful, somehow. Being looked at without being assessed for what you needed.

"No," she said. "Not really."

"Alright."

She drank her tea. It was plain, no honey, which was how she preferred it, though she hadn't told him that. Maybe he just didn't keep honey. Maybe he'd noticed from somewhere she couldn't account for.

"The engine's stable," she said.

"You said."

"It's been stable for three weeks. Since—" She stopped. "Since we finished." She turned the cup in her hands. "And I keep waiting to feel like that means something. Like it should feel different. Like I should feel—"

She didn't finish. The word she was looking for wasn't quite relief and wasn't quite triumph and wasn't quite anything she had a name for.

"Safe?" Dammon offered.

"Maybe." She considered it. "Or maybe just like something. Like I know what comes next."

The fire shifted again. Karlach watched it.

"I woke up every morning in Avernus thinking today might be it," she said. "And then the engine, and thinking — every morning, same thing. Today might be it. And you get used to that. You get to where it's just how mornings feel." She looked at the handle on the bench, the feathers-or-leaves. "Now I wake up and I don't know what today is. And I keep thinking I should be glad about that."

"But you're not."

"I'm glad," she said, firmly, because she was. "I just don't — I don't know what glad looks like when there's no deadline."

Dammon picked up the handle and turned it in the firelight. "I've been here three years," he said. "Came to the city to set up a proper shop. Spent the first six months certain someone was going to shut it down, run me out, burn it down, something." He set the piece on the bench. "Every morning thinking — today might be the day the city decides it doesn't want a tiefling smith."

"Did it get better?"

"Changed," he said. "I stopped thinking today might be the day. Started thinking what am I making today." He picked up the scribe. "Not the same thing as better, exactly. But it gave me something to do with the morning."

She thought about that. "What am I making today," she repeated.

"Or whatever the version of that is for you." He shrugged. "I don't know what that is. You probably do."

She probably didn't, but she didn't say so. She was still working on the question.

---

He went back to work, and she went back to watching.

The fire had settled into a steadier state, the log fully caught now, and the light was more even. She could see the forge properly — the layout of it, the organization she'd noted before but hadn't really looked at. The tools hung in order of size on the left wall. The anvil was positioned to catch the best of the light from the high window. The water barrel was close but not so close it was a hazard. There was a shelf along the back wall with finished pieces on it, some wrapped in cloth, some not, and she found herself cataloguing them without meaning to.

A set of hinges, large, decorative. A knife with a handle that matched the scribing style of the handle he was making now. Something that might have been a buckle. A small thing she couldn't identify from here — round, with something at the center.

"Can I look at the shelf?" she asked.

"Help yourself."

She crossed to it and examined things without touching, mostly, because she was being careful. The knife was beautiful in a plain way — no flourishes beyond the scribing, which was enough. The buckle was heavy, the kind that wouldn't fail under load. The round thing turned out to be a compass, the needle still moving faintly as she leaned close to it.

"You made a compass," she said.

"Someone commissioned it. Sailor. Wanted something that would last." He didn't look up from the work. "Took me two weeks to get the casing right. The needle's the easy part."

"I would have thought the needle was the hard part."

"The needle just has to be true. The casing has to protect it." He set down the scribe. "Different problems."

She looked at the compass for another moment, then went back to her stool. The tea was cooler now but still warm, and she drank the rest of it.

"Can I ask you something?" she said.

"You've been asking me things."

"Something different."

He set down the scribe. "Go ahead."

She turned the empty cup in her hands. "When you were first setting up. When you were waking up every morning thinking today might be the day. Did you ever—" She stopped, reformulated. "Did you ever think about what happened if it wasn't? If you just kept going and nothing happened? Did that feel like anything?"

He thought about it. She appreciated that he thought about it rather than reaching for the first available answer.

"Felt like waiting," he said finally. "For a while. Like I was always one step ahead of something that might not even be following me." He picked up the scribe again, turned it. "Then one day I realized I'd stopped checking over my shoulder. I didn't notice it happening. Just noticed it was done."

"And then?"

"And then I made a hinge." He almost smiled. "A very good hinge. Sold it to a builder on the harbor road. He came back a month later for six more." He set the scribe down. "That was a good day."

She sat with that.

The engine ticked. The fire roared. Outside, she could hear the street — carts, voices, the specific layered noise of a city going about its afternoon. She'd been in cities before. She'd grown up in one. But she'd spent ten years in a place where cities were something you remembered, not somewhere you lived, and she was still learning to hear Baldur's Gate as background rather than information.

"I keep thinking," she said, "that when the engine stops—"

She stopped. Started again.

"I keep thinking about who I am without it." She looked at her hands. "Like — the engine's been the thing. The problem to solve. The thing that might kill me. And it's been terrible, don't misunderstand me, I'm not saying I wanted it—" She stopped again, because she was starting to talk in circles. "It's just that having a thing that might kill you gives you a very clear metric for whether you're doing alright. And when it stops—"

"You won't have the metric," Dammon said.

"Right." She exhaled. "Right. And I don't know what I use instead."

He was quiet for a moment. The forge said everything the forge always said, which was everything and nothing.

"I don't think I'm the right person to answer that," he said.

"I know. I wasn't asking you to."

"I know you weren't." He picked up the handle again, the feathers-or-leaves. "But for what it's worth — you came in here today. You didn't have to. You didn't come in for six days and then you came in." He turned the piece over. "That's something."

"Walking through a door is something?"

"Depends on what's behind it." He set the piece down on the bench beside him, not on the far side, not put away, just — within reach. "Sometimes it is."

She looked at the handle. The scribing caught the light.

"Is that for me?" she asked.

He glanced at it. "Don't know yet," he said. "Might be."

"What would I do with a handle without a thing attached to it?"

"Hold it," he said. "If you wanted." He picked it up and held it out to her.

She took it — carefully, checked her palms first, the heat was low today, she'd been calm — and it sat in her hand the way it had before. The weight of it. The fine scribing under her thumb.

"It's good work," she said.

"It's not finished."

"It's still good work."

He didn't argue with that. She turned the handle in her hand, feeling the pattern, the way the lines branched. Feathers or leaves. She could feel both, depending on how she held it.

"The thing about the metric," Dammon said, going back to the anvil, picking up the light hammer, "is that you probably already have a new one. You just haven't named it yet."

"That's a very optimistic reading."

"I'm a tiefling smith in Baldur's Gate," he said. "I have to be practical about what I'm optimistic about." He looked at the anvil, not at her. "You came in today. You've been here — what, an hour?"

"Something like that."

"And you're still here."

She looked at the handle in her hands. "I'm still here," she agreed.

He set the hammer down, adjusted the piece on the anvil, picked the hammer back up. "That's not nothing," he said, and started working again.

---

The afternoon moved. She didn't track it precisely — the light from the high window shifted, the fire was fed twice, a boy came to the door with a delivery and Dammon signed for it without stopping work, just set the hammer down and signed and picked the hammer back up in one motion that was so practiced it was almost funny. She watched the light change on the walls. She watched his hands.

Her own hands were in her lap, the handle in them, and she wasn't doing anything with it except holding it. That seemed like enough.

She thought about Avernus. She did that less than she used to — the thoughts came less often, and when they came they were less sharp. She'd been told that would happen and hadn't quite believed it, but there it was. She thought about the ten years, the fights, the things she'd learned to do and the things she'd had to stop being in order to survive. She thought about coming back and finding that the city had changed and she had changed and neither of them had waited for the other.

She thought about the companions, scattered now to their various lives. Wyll and his father. Shadowheart and her parents. Astarion, doing whatever Astarion was doing, which she hoped was something he'd chosen. She thought about the brain, and the nautiloid, and the long road from the beach to here. She thought about the moment the engine had stabilized — not improved, just settled into its current state, the one that was sustainable for some definition of sustainable — and how she'd sat down on the floor of the inn room and just breathed for a while.

She hadn't told anyone about that. She'd been alone, which was fine, which was maybe the only way that moment could have happened. Just her and the engine and the floor and the breathing.

She thought about what Dammon had said. What am I making today.

She didn't know yet. But she was here. The handle was in her hands and the forge was loud and the afternoon was going on around her, and she was in it — not running from something, not running toward something, just in the middle of it, present, which was a thing she was still learning to be.

The handle was cool under her thumb. She traced the scribing. Feathers. Leaves. She could feel both.

Dammon set the hammer down and came to the water barrel again, and this time when he'd washed his face and dried his hands he pulled his stool closer to the anvil and sat, which was the first time she'd seen him sit since she arrived.

"Long day?" she asked.

"Long week." He rolled his right shoulder, a slow rotation. "That commission's due day after tomorrow and I'm fighting the metal."

"Fighting it how?"

"It wants to be thicker than the design calls for." He looked at the piece on the anvil, which had taken a different shape now — she'd been watching it change all afternoon but hadn't tried to name what it was becoming. "I could change the design. Or I could keep arguing."

"Which are you going to do?"

"Keep arguing," he said. "Probably." He looked at the piece. "The design's right. The metal just needs convincing."

She looked at the piece. It had a curve to it now, a sweep she recognized as the beginning of something, though she still couldn't say what. "Is that how you think about it? Convincing?"

"How else would you think about it?"

"Forcing," she said. "Breaking. Bending."

He shook his head. "Force breaks. Convincing holds." He picked up the hammer, set it back down. "You heat it right, strike it right, it goes where you want. But you have to understand what it wants first. Otherwise you're just hitting a thing."

She thought about that. "What does this one want?"

"To be a bowl," he said. "I think. I wasn't sure this morning but I'm more sure now." He tilted his head. "Not the commission. Different piece. That one's—" He gestured at the anvil. "That one's a bracket. Structural. For a building on the harbor."

"So you're making two things."

"Usually making three or four things. You start something, you set it aside, you come back." He looked at the bowl-that-was-becoming. "This one's been sitting for a week. I pick it up when I need to think about something else."

She looked at the handle in her hands. "Is this one of them? The ones you pick up when you need to think?"

"Started it this morning," he said. "After I saw you walk past again."

She didn't say anything. The fire said what it always said.

"I wasn't sure if you'd come in," he said. "But I thought if you did, you should have something to hold."

She looked down at the handle. The scribing under her thumb, the weight of it, the way it sat in her palm without requiring anything from her. Just there. Just something to hold.

"You made this for me," she said. Not a question.

"I made it this morning," he said. "Didn't know what it was for. Now I think I do."

She turned it over. The other side of the scribing was different — not feathers or leaves but something she couldn't quite name, a pattern that was almost writing but wasn't, that felt like it meant something without being legible. "What's on this side?"

"Something I learned in Elturel," he said. "Before." He didn't look away from the fire. "It's not a word. It's a mark. Old craft tradition. You put it on something you're giving away so the work stays with the thing."

"The work stays with it?"

"So it knows what it is." He shrugged, a little self-conscious. "I know that's—"

"No," she said. "I like that."

He nodded, not looking at her.

She closed her fingers around the handle. The weight of it. The work in it — she could feel the work in it, the care that had gone into the scribing, the deliberate shaping of the grip so it sat right in a hand. He'd looked at her hands this morning, she realized. When she'd come in. He'd looked at her hands and then he'd made this.

"Thank you," she said.

"It's not finished," he said. "I'll need to fit it to something. If you want."

"What would you fit it to?"

He thought about it. "Don't know yet. What do you need?"

She almost said nothing, because that was her first instinct and her first instinct was usually wrong about things like this. She sat with it instead, thought about the cups she'd broken and the carving she'd stopped doing and the mornings she didn't know what to do with.

"Something to make," she said. "I think."

He nodded. "Then I'll fit it to something to make with."

She didn't know what that meant exactly, and she didn't ask, because she thought it was probably the kind of thing that would become clear in the making. That was how it worked, maybe. You started with a shape and found out what it was later.

The fire shifted. The afternoon light through the high window had gone golden, which meant it was later than she'd thought, which meant she'd been here for — she did the rough calculation and was surprised by the result.

"I should go," she said.

"You should eat something," he said. "There's a decent place two streets over."

"Are you—" She stopped. "Are you offering to eat with me, or are you telling me where to eat?"

He considered. "Both, maybe. If you want company."

She thought about the inn room, which was fine, which was hers, which she could go back to and close the door and be alone in a way that was sometimes restful and sometimes not. She thought about the forge, and the afternoon, and the handle in her hand.

"Alright," she said.

He stood, began banking the fire with the efficiency of long habit. She watched him and thought about what Dammon had said — not the part about the metric, though she was still thinking about that, but the other part. That she'd come in today. That she was still here.

She was still here.

The engine ticked, low and steady. The forge cooled by degrees. Outside, the city was going into its evening, all that layered noise shifting register as the afternoon traders went home and the evening ones came out. She could hear it changing even through the forge walls.

She stood, put her stool back in the corner. She kept the handle.

Dammon pulled on a coat, extinguished the lamps one by one, and came to where she was standing. He was taller than she usually remembered — she was tall herself, but he had a few inches on her, and up close the burn scars along his jaw were just part of his face, not a thing to note particularly, just part of what he looked like.

"Ready?" he said.

She thought about that word. She thought about the six days of walking past and the hour she'd spent watching him work and the handle in her hand and the afternoon that had gone somewhere she hadn't expected.

"Getting there," she said.

He opened the door and the evening came in — cooler than the forge, smelling of the city, of food from somewhere nearby and the damp of the harbor. She stepped through it, and he pulled the door closed behind them, and the forge went quiet at their backs.

She put the handle in her pocket, where it sat with its good weight against her hip.

The city was loud in a different way than the forge — not a roar but a murmur, a thousand separate sounds that added up to something she was still learning to hear as home. She walked into it beside Dammon, who knew where he was going, and she matched her stride to his, and the evening opened up around them like something that hadn't decided what it was yet.

That was alright. She was getting used to that.
