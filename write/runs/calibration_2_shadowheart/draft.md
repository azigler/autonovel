The temple smelled like plaster dust and tallow candles, which Shadowheart had decided was the smell of reconstruction. Everything in the outer districts smelled like it lately — that particular combination of old smoke and new effort, the city trying to convince itself that the damage could be undone with enough lime mortar and good intentions.

She was copying a list of names onto a clean sheet when the door opened without a knock.

"I brought food."

Karlach filled the doorway the way she filled most spaces — not just physically, though that was part of it, the horns and the height and the particular quality of warmth that radiated off her in the literal sense. She was carrying a canvas bag in each hand and wearing an expression that suggested she expected to be argued with.

Shadowheart set down her pen. "I didn't ask for food."

"No, you didn't." Karlach stepped inside, ducking her chin slightly for the doorframe even though she didn't quite need to. "That's kind of the point. Move those — " she nodded at the stack of supply ledgers taking up half the small table — "I've got bread that's still warm and I'm not setting it on the floor."

There was a beat where Shadowheart considered pointing out that this was her workspace, that she had been in the middle of something, that she hadn't invited anyone. The list of names waited on the desk, half-finished.

She moved the ledgers.

Karlach spread things out with the efficiency of someone who had fed herself from whatever could be foraged or bought in a hurry for most of her adult life: bread, yes, still faintly warm through the cloth it was wrapped in, and a wedge of hard cheese, and some dried figs, and a small ceramic pot of something that smelled like spiced lentils.

"Sit down," Karlach said.

"I was sitting."

"Sit down and eat, then."

Shadowheart sat. She pulled the bread toward her more because it smelled good than because she had any intention of conceding the argument. "Who told you I wasn't eating."

"Doesn't matter."

"It was Wyll."

Karlach's mouth twitched. "It was Wyll," she confirmed, and settled herself onto the bench across the table, which creaked under her in a way that suggested the temple's furnishings had not been built with tiefling barbarians in mind. "He said you've been here since the fifth bell most mornings and you're not stopping for meals."

"I stop for meals."

"When?"

Shadowheart tore a piece of bread. The crust gave with a sound she felt in her back teeth. "When I'm hungry."

"That's not the same thing." Karlach opened the pot of lentils and pushed it across the table. "Eat. Tell me what you're working on."

It wasn't a deflection, exactly. Karlach asked about work the way she asked about everything — with the same directness, no hierarchy between the important questions and the ordinary ones. Shadowheart found herself answering.

"Supply inventory. There are twelve families in the Steeps who lost their stores when the building came down — not the fire damage, the structural collapse from the shockwave. The city's relief distribution keeps missing them because their address doesn't exist in the records anymore. The building doesn't exist anymore." She dipped bread into the lentils without thinking about it. "I'm trying to document them well enough that they don't fall through again."

Karlach was quiet for a moment. "And you're the one doing that because—"

"Because I'm here and someone has to." Shadowheart heard how it came out. "The temple has three clergy and they're managing the distribution site on the main road. This is the part that doesn't fit their workflow."

Karlach nodded slowly. She had poured herself nothing, Shadowheart noticed, just sat with her forearms on the table and watched Shadowheart eat with an expression that was trying very hard not to look satisfied.

"You're watching me eat," Shadowheart said.

"I'm not."

"You're making the face."

"I don't have a face."

"You have several faces. This is the one that means you were right about something and you're being gracious about it." Shadowheart took another piece of bread. "It's annoying."

Karlach laughed — the big one, the one that came from somewhere in her chest and didn't apologize for taking up room. The candle on the desk wavered. "Okay. Fair. I was a little worried."

"I know." Shadowheart looked at the lentils. They were good — cumin, she thought, and something slightly sharp she couldn't name. "Thank you."

The words came out more simply than she'd intended. Karlach just nodded, like it wasn't worth making a thing of.

They ate. Outside, someone was hammering something three buildings over, a regular rhythm that had become so constant Shadowheart barely registered it anymore. The candle settled. The list of names waited.

"I need to make a delivery this afternoon," Shadowheart said, eventually. "Supplies for one of the families on that list. Someone from the main distribution was supposed to bring it yesterday and didn't."

"I'll come."

"You don't have to."

"I know." Karlach reached for a fig. "But I've been walking around the docks for three days trying to figure out what to do with myself and I'm going slowly mad, so." She bit the fig in half. "Unless you'd rather go alone."

Shadowheart considered it honestly. She had been going alone to most things since she arrived in the city, by habit and by something that wasn't quite preference. The habit of solitude was deep in her, older than Shar's strictures even — or maybe the strictures had made it deeper. Hard to know, now. Hard to separate what had been chosen from what had been shaped.

"No," she said. "You can come."

---

The supplies were packed into a handcart that one of the temple volunteers had left in the side yard — flour, salt, dried beans, a small jar of oil. Shadowheart checked the list against the contents twice, then a third time, because the family had four children and she had written down four children and she wanted the numbers to be right.

Karlach waited without fidgeting, which was unusual for her. She stood in the yard with her face tipped up toward the afternoon sun, eyes closed, and just — stood there. Like the sun was something to pay attention to.

"Ready?" Shadowheart said.

"Mm." Karlach opened her eyes. They caught the light. "Sorry. It's just — I spent so long underground. Or in Avernus where the light is this horrible red-brown color that makes everything look like it's on fire. Real sun still gets me sometimes."

She said it plainly, without the particular weight that meant she was looking for a response. Just noting it.

"Let's go," Shadowheart said.

The outer districts had a particular texture to them, different from the lower city's older density. Here the streets were wider and more irregular, the buildings newer or at least newer-looking, the damage from the battle more visible because there had been less to begin with. They passed a block where half the structures were still standing and half were rubble with colored flags marking the safe perimeter, and a crew of three people picking through the stone with careful hands.

Karlach watched them as they passed. "Salvage?"

"Mostly. Some of the stone is good. And people want their things back if there's anything to find."

"Hm." A pause. "Do you know any of them? The families."

"Not personally. I have names." Shadowheart navigated around a patch of broken paving. The handcart's wheel was slightly wobbly and she had to correct for it every few steps. "The mother's name is Drina. The father died in the collapse. She has four children, the eldest is eleven."

Karlach absorbed this without comment. They walked another half-block before she said, "How do you do that? Just — show up with supplies for someone you've never met."

"You put things in a cart and you bring them to the address." Shadowheart glanced at her. "Was that a philosophical question?"

"Kind of." Karlach caught the cart's handle from the other side without asking, taking some of the weight. "I mean — I'm trying to figure out how any of this works. The normal life stuff. Showing up for people you don't know. I've spent so long where useful meant — " she gestured vaguely with her free hand — "kill the thing. Stop the other thing from killing someone. Very clear metric for useful."

"And now."

"And now I'm walking around the docks feeling like I should be doing something and not knowing what." She said it without self-pity, just the flat assessment of a problem. "Wyll's been helping coordinate the reconstruction crews. Gale's been doing something with the Weave, some kind of structural assessment thing. Even Astarion's apparently been — I don't know, charming people into donating to something. It's very on-brand."

Shadowheart almost smiled. "What have you been doing?"

"Lifting things." Karlach paused. "When there's things to lift. Which there isn't always." Another pause. "I've been eating a lot. The food here is genuinely incredible, I want you to know that. I had a pastry yesterday that I thought about for four hours."

"That's not nothing."

"It's not useful."

"Karlach." Shadowheart stopped walking. The handcart jolted slightly. "You know your engine is still — "

"I know." Karlach's voice was level. "I know what it is. I'm not pretending it's fine. I just — " she started walking again, and Shadowheart fell back into step — "I don't want to spend whatever time I have waiting. That's all. I want to be doing something."

The sun was warm on the left side of Shadowheart's face. The hammering from earlier had stopped, replaced by a more distant sound, voices carrying over rooftops.

"This is something," she said, meaning the cart, the delivery, the afternoon.

"Yeah." Karlach looked at the street ahead. "I know."

They turned onto a narrower road that sloped slightly downward toward the lower city. The buildings here were closer together, casting longer shadows, and the air changed quality — less dust, more cooksmoke, the particular smell of close-quarters living that Shadowheart associated with the Steeps even though she'd only been in this part of the city for a few weeks.

She had grown up in Shar's halls, which had their own smell. Stone and incense and cold air. No cooksmoke. No children's voices.

She caught herself reaching for the familiar internal address — the particular shape of a prayer, the way her thoughts would organize themselves around an invocation before she was fully conscious of doing it. The muscle memory of faith. She had done it a hundred times in the past month and each time there was the same small lurch, like a step that ended before she expected it to.

Not hers. Not anymore. Or — not right, rather. The prayers to Shar were still there, grooved into her like a path worn into stone, but they led somewhere she had chosen to leave. She wasn't sure yet what replaced them.

Karlach said, "You went quiet."

"I was thinking."

"About?"

A beat. The honest answer was complicated. "Prayer. The habit of it."

Karlach didn't push. She waited, which was its own kind of question.

"I keep reaching for it," Shadowheart said. "Shar's liturgy. Before I've thought about it. And then I catch myself and — " she moved the cart around a broken cobble — "it's like reaching for a door and finding a wall. Which is what I chose. But the reaching keeps happening."

"Does it hurt?"

The question was so direct it almost caught her off guard. "Not exactly. It's more like — disorientation. I knew who I was inside that structure. What I was for." She heard how that sounded. "That's not a defense of it. I know what it cost."

"I wasn't going to say it was."

"I know." Shadowheart looked at the street. "I just — I'm aware of how it sounds. To miss the shape of something that was also a cage."

Karlach was quiet for several steps. "When I got back from Avernus," she said finally, "the first few weeks, I kept waking up before dawn because that's when the drills started. Even when I didn't have to. Even when there was nothing to drill for." She shifted her grip on the cart. "Your body learns things. Doesn't mean you wanted to be there."

Shadowheart absorbed this. It wasn't the same thing, exactly, but it was close enough to the shape of the thing that she felt it land somewhere.

"What did you do? With the mornings."

"Mostly just — sat with it. Watched the sun come up. Ate breakfast when there was breakfast." A small shrug. "Tried not to treat every moment like I was about to be assigned something." She paused. "Didn't always work. Still doesn't, honestly."

"But you kept doing it."

"What else are you going to do." It wasn't rhetorical — Karlach said it with genuine curiosity, like she was still working out the answer.

They came to the address — a narrow building wedged between a chandler's shop and what had been a laundry before the shockwave took out its front wall. The building itself was standing, which was something. There were marks on the door frame in chalk, the kind the relief workers used to indicate assessed and occupied.

Shadowheart knocked.

A pause. Then footsteps, fast and uneven, and the door opened on a boy of maybe eight or nine who looked at them both with the particular calculating stare of a child who has learned not to assume good news.

"We have a delivery for Drina," Shadowheart said. "From the temple on Cutter's Road."

The boy looked at the cart. Then at Karlach, who was very large and had horns, and who was smiling at him with the careful warmth of someone who knew she could be alarming.

"Hi," Karlach said. "Is your mum home?"

The boy's stare held for another moment. Then he turned and shouted "Mum!" into the interior of the building with the full volume of someone who saw no reason to modulate themselves, and disappeared back inside.

A woman appeared at the door — mid-thirties, probably, though she looked tired in a way that added years. She took in the cart, and Shadowheart, and Karlach, in that order.

"The temple sent you," she said.

"Yes. I'm sorry it took longer than it should have." Shadowheart gestured at the cart. "Flour, salt, dried beans, oil. There should be enough here for several weeks. And — " she produced the list from her coat pocket — "I've updated the relief records to include your current address. You should be in the next distribution cycle without needing to register again."

The woman looked at the list. Her expression was hard to read — not ungrateful, just tired, the particular exhaustion of someone who has spent too long navigating systems that were not designed with her in mind.

"The last person said they'd fix the records," she said.

"I know. I'm the one who fixed them." Shadowheart kept her voice even. "If there's a problem with the next cycle, ask for me specifically. Shadowheart, Cutter's Road temple."

A pause. The woman's expression shifted slightly — something that might have been the beginning of relief, if she let herself feel it.

"All right," she said. "Thank you."

From inside the building came the sound of at least three children arguing about something, and the boy's voice rising above the others with fresh outrage. The woman closed her eyes for a moment.

"Drina," Karlach said, "do you need anything moved? Carried? I have — " she gestured at herself — "a lot of structural integrity, if that's useful."

The woman blinked at her. Then, unexpectedly, she almost laughed — a short, surprised sound. "The shelving in the back room collapsed. I've been stacking things on the floor."

"Show me," Karlach said.

Shadowheart helped carry the supplies inside while Karlach disappeared into the back of the building with Drina, and within a few minutes there was the sound of furniture being moved with the particular ease of someone for whom furniture presented no physical obstacle. The boy materialized at Shadowheart's elbow while she stacked flour sacks.

"Is she a demon?" he asked.

"She's a tiefling." Shadowheart set down a sack. "There's a difference."

He considered this. "Her horns are bigger than the other tieflings."

"Yes."

"Is that good?"

"She seems to think so." Shadowheart looked at him. He had his mother's tired eyes but he was still looking at things with curiosity, which seemed like something worth noting. "What's your name?"

"Pell."

"Pell. Do you know how to write?"

A suspicious look. "Some."

"If you want, I can leave some paper and a pencil. It's useful to be able to write your address down — for the relief records, and for other things." She paused. "You don't have to."

He thought about it with the gravity of an eight-year-old considering a significant decision. "Okay," he said.

She found the paper in her coat pocket — she had started carrying it — and tore off a sheet. She wrote the address at the top in large letters and handed it to him. He took it with both hands.

From the back room, something heavy landed with a thud that shook the floor slightly, followed by Karlach's voice saying, "That's fine, it's fine, the shelf is fine," and Drina's surprised laugh.

Pell looked toward the sound, then back at the paper. "She's loud," he said.

"She is," Shadowheart agreed.

"I like it," he said, with the simple authority of someone who had decided.

---

They walked back in the late afternoon light, the handcart empty now, its wobbly wheel making a slightly different sound without the weight. The sun had moved to the right angle where it caught the dust in the air and made everything look warmer than it was.

"You were good with him," Karlach said. "The boy."

"I gave him a piece of paper."

"You asked his name first."

Shadowheart let that sit. The wheel wobbled. She corrected for it.

"I keep wondering," she said, "what I would have been like. If I'd grown up differently." She hadn't meant to say it out loud, exactly — it was the kind of thought she'd been having in the early mornings when the temple was quiet and the prayers she reached for weren't there. "Shar's training started early. I don't have a lot of — before."

Karlach glanced at her. "Before the training?"

"Before the doctrine. Before the structure of it." She moved the cart around a corner. "I know I liked music. I know I didn't like being cold. Those feel like mine. Most of the rest of it—" she made a small gesture — "I can't always tell what was me and what was shaped."

"Does that scare you?"

"It would be easier if it did." She considered. "Mostly it makes me curious. Which is strange. I expected to feel — I don't know. More grief. And there's grief, but underneath it there's this—" she stopped, looking for the word.

"Space?" Karlach offered.

"Space," Shadowheart agreed. "Yes. Which is alarming in its own way. I'm not sure what to put in it."

The street widened slightly, opening onto a small square where a fountain was being repaired — the basin had cracked, and someone had patched it with fresh mortar that hadn't quite dried yet, the smell of it sharp and mineral. Two pigeons were investigating the dry basin with professional skepticism.

Karlach stopped walking. Shadowheart stopped too.

"Can I say something," Karlach said, "that might sound strange."

"You rarely warn me before saying things."

"I'm trying to be careful." She was looking at the fountain, not at Shadowheart. "I think — I think the space being there is the point. Not, like, a problem to solve." She turned then, and there was something in her face that was more careful than her usual directness — measured, chosen. "I spent so long in Avernus being useful. Defined by what I could do, what I could kill, what I could survive. And when I got out I kept waiting to feel like myself again, like there was a self waiting somewhere that the fighting had covered up." A pause. "But I don't think that's how it works. I think the space is where you find out."

Shadowheart looked at her. Karlach's eyes were steady, and the late light caught the warm undertone of her skin and the faint shimmer of heat that always surrounded her, and she looked — present, was the word. Fully present, in a way that was occasionally uncomfortable to be the object of.

"Find out what," Shadowheart said.

"What you actually want. When nobody's telling you." A small shrug, the kind that meant she was saying something real. "What you'd choose if choosing was an option."

The pigeons had given up on the fountain and moved to a more promising crack in the paving. The mortar smell was fading. Shadowheart looked at the patched basin and thought about the list of names on her desk, the way she'd checked it three times, the way it had mattered to her that the numbers were right.

"I chose to spare the Nightsong," she said. "I knew what it meant when I did it. I knew I was choosing — not just against Shar, but against everything I'd been built to want." She paused. "And I'd do it again. So I suppose that's something."

"That's a lot of things," Karlach said quietly.

They stood there for a moment in the small square, the empty cart between them, the city going about its reconstruction around them.

"I don't know what I believe now," Shadowheart said. "About gods. About faith. I keep — there are moments when I want to address something, when something happens and I want to offer it up somewhere, and I don't know where to send it." She heard the admission in this and did not take it back. "It's possible I'm not meant for belief without an object."

"Or maybe you're figuring out what the object is." Karlach picked up the cart handle again. "That's allowed to take a while."

They started walking. The shadows were longer now, the afternoon tilting toward evening, and the outer districts were quieting around them in the way of places that work hard and rest early.

"You said you've been walking the docks," Shadowheart said, after a while.

"Yeah."

"Is that useful? In the sense you meant."

Karlach thought about it. "No. But the fish market opens at the fifth bell and I've been watching them bring in the boats. There's this one old man who has the same argument with the same buyer every single morning about the price of eels, and every morning they end up agreeing on the same number, and they both act like it's a victory." She shook her head. "I've watched it four days in a row. I don't know why but it makes me feel better."

Shadowheart considered this. "That sounds like something."

"It's an old man and some eels."

"It sounds like you found something that makes you feel better and you've been going back to it." She looked at the road ahead. "That's not nothing."

Karlach was quiet for half a block. "Huh," she said.

"I'm occasionally insightful."

"No, I know that, I just—" Karlach laughed, shorter this time, more private. "Yeah. Okay. Maybe."

The temple came back into view, the pale stone of its front wall catching the last of the direct light. The scaffolding on the east side was still up — they were re-pointing the mortar on the upper courses, and the work went slowly because there were only two people who knew how to do it properly. Shadowheart had been watching the scaffolding for three weeks and it never seemed to come down, which she had started to find oddly comforting. Some things took as long as they took.

She brought the cart to a stop in the side yard. Karlach stood in the yard with her hands in her pockets, not leaving yet, which Shadowheart noted without comment.

"Thank you," Shadowheart said. "For the food. And the shelving."

"The shelving was the fun part." Karlach grinned. "You should have seen her face when I just — picked it up. Like it was nothing. Which it was, but still."

"Drina looked like she hadn't been surprised by something good in a while."

"Yeah." The grin faded to something softer. "The kid was great. Pell. He asked me if I was a demon."

"He asked me the same thing."

"What did you tell him?"

"That there was a difference. Between demon and tiefling."

Karlach tilted her head. "And what did he say?"

"He said he liked your horns."

Karlach laughed again — the big one, the chest one, the one that made the candle waver. "Excellent taste," she said. "That kid has excellent taste."

The light was going. The scaffolding threw a long shadow across the yard. Shadowheart looked at the door to the temple and then at Karlach, who was still standing there with her hands in her pockets and her face open in the particular way it was when she wasn't working at anything.

"Come back tomorrow," Shadowheart said. "If you want. There are more deliveries."

Karlach's expression shifted — not surprise, exactly, but something that registered the offer for what it was. "Yeah?"

"The cart wheel needs fixing and I don't know how to fix it." She paused. "And the company was — adequate."

"Adequate." Karlach repeated it with great solemnity. "High praise."

"Don't make it strange."

"I'm not making it strange." But she was smiling, and it was the smaller smile, the real one. "Yeah. I'll come back. Fifth bell?"

"Sixth," Shadowheart said. "I'm not getting up at the fifth bell for you."

"Sixth bell it is." Karlach picked up the canvas bags she'd left by the door — she'd forgotten them, apparently, or hadn't bothered to take them when she went to help with the shelving. She slung them over one shoulder. "Eat the rest of those lentils tonight."

"I was going to."

"Sure you were."

Shadowheart watched her go — out through the side gate, onto the street, her silhouette catching the last of the light before the angle of the building cut it off. The warmth she radiated was visible, faintly, as a shimmer in the cooling air. Then she was gone.

The yard was quiet. The scaffolding creaked once, settling.

Shadowheart stood there for a moment, not reaching for anything in particular. Just standing in the yard with the empty cart and the smell of mortar and the evening coming in. There was the list of names waiting on her desk. There was the rest of the lentils. There was tomorrow, with its sixth-bell arrival and its deliveries and whatever else the city produced that needed doing.

She went inside.

The candle was still burning on the desk, lower now, and the list of names was where she'd left it. She sat down. She read over what she'd written. The names were still there, all twelve of them, and the addresses, and the notes about what each family needed.

She picked up the pen.

Outside, someone had started hammering again — a different rhythm this time, slower, more deliberate. She listened to it for a moment. Then she went back to the list, and the pen moved, and the evening settled around the temple like something that had been waiting patiently for her to stop resisting it.
