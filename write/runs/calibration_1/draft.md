**still burning**

*A post-game character study. Astarion can't sleep. Karlach can't cool down. A campfire that's mostly ash, and two people who've survived things they can't talk about yet.*

*Heads up — this one sits with some quiet stuff around the body as a site of memory. Nothing graphic, but Astarion's history is present in the subtext, and Karlach's engine is front and center. Take care of yourselves.*

---

The fire had burned down to almost nothing.

Not quite nothing. There was still heat in the coals — Astarion could feel it from three feet away, a soft, sourceless warmth that didn't ask anything of him. He stood at the edge of the camp for a moment, watching the orange pulse of it, and then he sat down in the grass because there was nowhere else to go.

This kept happening.

Not sleep — he'd had 200 years of not sleeping, the body had no particular use for it — but the lying down. The closing of eyes. The performance of rest that sometimes, in the weeks since Baldur's Gate, became something almost like rest, and then snapped shut again. Tonight it had lasted perhaps an hour before he was standing at his tent's entrance with no clear memory of how he'd gotten there, the night air on his face, his hands at his sides.

The camp was quiet. It was always quiet after the brain, after the city, after everyone had exhaled collectively and begun the slow process of figuring out what came next. Wyll had his father now, and that took up considerable space in the emotional landscape of any given evening. Shadowheart moved through each day with a particular careful attention, like someone learning to walk on a new leg. Gale had gone back to Waterdeep twice already.

Astarion stayed. He wasn't entirely sure why.

He stretched his legs out toward the coals and looked up. The sky was doing something interesting — a faint smear of cloud across the stars, backlit by a moon that was most of the way to full. He catalogued it without meaning to, the way he catalogued most things: filed under *aesthetically useful, possibly worth mentioning if the occasion arose.* Old habit. Everything had been a potential tool for 200 years. He was still working on the habit of looking at things without immediately assessing their utility.

The ember nearest his foot shifted. Settled.

"You're doing it again."

He turned.

Karlach was sitting on the other side of the fire pit — had been sitting there, he realized, the whole time. She was cross-legged in the grass, her elbows on her knees, her chin in one hand. In the dark she was mostly silhouette, but the coals threw just enough light to catch the faint lines of heat-shimmer rising from her shoulders.

"Doing what," he said.

"The staring. The—" she made a gesture with her free hand, vague, encompassing. "The sitting very still and looking at things like you're deciding whether they're dangerous."

"I'm looking at the sky."

"Yeah." She didn't say anything else for a moment. "I've been watching you do it for about ten minutes."

"Watching me," he repeated, "from the dark, without announcing yourself. And I'm the one being strange."

"Didn't want to interrupt." She shrugged one shoulder. "You had a whole thing going."

He looked at her. She was watching the coals now, not him — the orange light moving in her eyes, the particular stillness she got sometimes that didn't look like her usual stillness. Karlach in motion was a force of nature, all volume and forward momentum. Karlach at rest, genuinely at rest, was something quieter. He'd noticed it before. She wore it like a different garment.

"Why are you awake," he said.

"Why are *you* awake?"

"I asked first."

She made a sound that wasn't quite a laugh. "Too hot," she said. "Couldn't — it gets worse when I lie still. Feels like I'm cooking from the inside." A pause. "Which, I mean. I am. But it's easier not to think about when I'm moving."

He nodded once. He understood that — the logic of motion, of keeping enough surface area occupied that nothing could settle into the spaces underneath. He'd spent 200 years being very good at it.

"And you?" she said.

He considered several answers. Settled on: "Habit."

Karlach turned her head to look at him. He could see her deciding whether to push, watching him the way she sometimes did — with that directness that wasn't unkind, just very straightforward, like a door that opened outward instead of inward.

She didn't push. She looked back at the fire.

"Habit," she said, as if she were tasting it. "Yeah. Okay."

---

The thing about the camp, in the aftermath, was that it had no particular reason to exist anymore.

They'd won. The Netherbrain was gone. Baldur's Gate was standing. The tadpoles were gone, the Dead Three's plans were ash, the nautiloid was wreckage at the bottom of the sea or scattered across some gods-forsaken field — he wasn't sure, he hadn't asked. They had won, and then they had set up camp outside the city because nobody had quite managed to say *so, now what,* and the camp had simply continued, day after day, held together by inertia and the fact that none of them seemed to know where else to go.

He'd thought about going. He thought about it regularly, in the abstract — *I could leave.* He could go to Baldur's Gate, find a townhouse, drink his way through a social season, build something new from all the nothing Cazador had left him. He could go east. He could go anywhere.

He was still here.

The grass under his hands was cool and slightly damp with evening dew. He pressed his palms flat against it, feeling the chill work up through his fingers. He'd always run cold. He was running colder now.

"Can I ask you something," Karlach said.

"You can ask."

"Does it—" She stopped. Started again. "The whole thing. The not sleeping. Is that—" Another stop. He waited. "Sorry. I'm trying to figure out how to ask this without it being weird."

"It's going to be weird regardless. Ask."

She huffed a short breath. "Does it bother you? That you can't — that your body just. Doesn't do that."

He thought about it. Genuinely thought about it, which was not something he always did when people asked questions about his body — usually the answer was already loaded and aimed before the question finished leaving someone's mouth.

"No," he said, and then, because that wasn't quite right: "Not the way you mean. I don't miss sleep. I don't remember missing it." He paused. "What bothers me is the lying there. The pretending."

"Why do you pretend?"

"Because—" He stopped. Because it was what you did, when other people slept. You lay down. You closed your eyes. You didn't make anyone uncomfortable by being upright and alert in the dark. He'd spent 200 years making himself small and invisible in whatever ways Cazador's rules allowed, and the body apparently had opinions about continuing that, regardless of whether the rules still applied.

He didn't say any of that. He said: "Old habit."

Karlach made a sound — low, acknowledging. Not the sound of someone who hadn't understood. The sound of someone who had understood and wasn't going to make him say the rest of it.

He glanced at her. She was looking at her hands, turning them over in the ember-light, studying the lines of her palms with the focused attention of someone who'd found something worth examining.

"The engine," she said, after a moment. Not a question.

"Mm."

"It's — I don't know if it's worse now or if I'm just noticing it more." She flattened her hands on her thighs. "In Avernus there was always so much noise. Fighting and fire and — you just didn't hear yourself. And then here, everything's so *quiet,* and all I can hear is—" She tapped her sternum twice, lightly. The sound it made wasn't quite a thud.

He'd heard it before — the tick of her engine, audible if you were close enough and the camp was still enough. He'd never said anything about it.

"Does it hurt?" he asked.

"Sometimes. Not — not like a wound hurts. More like." She turned her hands back over. "You know when you've been holding something too tight and you let go, and your hand aches? Like that. But it doesn't stop."

He looked at the coals. The pulse of orange-red in the dark, the heat still coming off them in steady waves.

"I keep thinking," Karlach said, "that there's going to be a moment when my body figures out that I'm safe. Like it'll just — click over. And I'll stop waiting for the next thing."

"Has it?"

"No." She laughed, short and without much humor. "You?"

He looked at her. "What do you think."

"I think you've been sitting like you're ready to run since you sat down."

He looked at his own hands. He was, he realized, braced against the ground — palms down, weight forward, knees slightly bent. The posture of someone who had calculated the three fastest exits from this location and was keeping them in peripheral awareness. He consciously relaxed his shoulders and felt how much effort that took.

"No," he said. "It hasn't clicked over."

---

Somewhere in the dark past the tree line, something small moved through the underbrush. Both of them looked up at the same moment. Listened. The sound settled into the ordinary category — bird, or rabbit, or some other creature with its own nocturnal business — and they both looked back at the fire.

"Can I ask *you* something," he said.

"Turnabout," she said. "Fair enough."

"When you were — in Avernus." He chose the words carefully, not because he was trying to be gentle but because he was trying to be accurate. "Did you—" He stopped, restructured. "You said you were fighting. The whole time."

"Pretty much."

"For Zariel."

"Yeah." Her voice didn't change on the word, but something in her posture did — a slight shift, a pulling inward. "I mean, I didn't know it was — I thought I'd signed up to fight, and I was fighting, so. For a while I just. Did the thing."

"And when you figured it out."

"Kept doing the thing." She said it simply, without bitterness. Like a fact. "Because what else was I going to do. I was in the hells. I couldn't — there wasn't a door I could just walk out of."

He understood that. The grammar of it, specifically. *There wasn't a door.* Not: *I was too afraid to leave.* Not: *I chose to stay.* There wasn't a door. The structure of the situation, rather than any failure of the person inside it.

He'd spent a long time trying to find the right way to think about his own 200 years. Cazador's house had plenty of doors. He'd known where they were. He'd walked through them every night on Cazador's errands, and he'd walked back in, and the question of why was something he'd been turning over for weeks now, in the small hours, in the quiet that wouldn't quite resolve into rest.

"Did you ever—" He stopped.

"Spit it out," Karlach said, not unkindly.

"Did you ever stop being angry about it. And then feel — strange about having stopped."

A silence. Not uncomfortable — Karlach's silences never really were, she wore them the way she wore everything, openly, without apology.

"Yeah," she said. "Like you've been carrying something so long your arms just keep being out." She held her arms slightly away from her body, demonstrating. "And you put it down and you're standing there like an idiot with your arms out."

"And someone asks what you're holding."

"And you have to say *nothing,* and it sounds wrong." She lowered her arms. "Yeah."

The coal nearest the center of the pit crumbled slightly, settling into itself. The light shifted, dimmed a fraction.

"I keep thinking I should feel—" He paused, choosing. "More different. Than I do."

Karlach looked at him.

"He's dead," Astarion said. "Cazador is dead. I'm—" He looked at his hands, the particular pale of them in the dark. "I'm free, ostensibly. And I wake up—" He corrected himself: "I get up, and my first thought is still. Threat assessment. Every morning. What does he want. What's the angle. And then I remember there is no angle, there's no—" A short exhale. "And then I have to spend a moment being surprised by that. Still. Every time."

Karlach was quiet for a moment. "How long?"

"Two hundred years." He said it the way he always said it — with a light, almost careless inflection, because saying it any other way made it too large to hold in a single breath. "Two hundred years of waking up and knowing exactly what the day required. What was permitted. What the consequences were for stepping outside what was permitted."

"And now."

"Now there are no consequences." He looked at the sky. "Which is — I know what it should feel like. Freedom. I know the word. I've used the word, I've thought about it for two hundred years, I had very specific ideas about what it would feel like." He paused. "It's strange. Not bad strange, I think. Just — the shape of it is different than I expected."

"What shape did you expect?"

He thought about it. "Lighter," he said finally. "I expected to feel lighter."

Karlach made a soft sound. Not a laugh. Something more like recognition.

"I thought when I got back," she said, "I'd feel like myself. Like — the real me, the one I was before Avernus. I had this whole idea of her. She was going to step right back in, and everything Avernus did was just going to—" She made a gesture, dispersing smoke. "But I got back and I was still me. The same me. With the engine and the everything." She touched her sternum again, a habit, checking. "And I kept waiting for the other version to show up."

"Has she?"

"I think—" Karlach paused. "I think maybe this *is* her. I think maybe I was waiting for someone who doesn't exist anymore, and this is just." She spread her hands, indicating herself, the fire, the dark around them. "This is what's left."

He looked at her. The ember-light on her face, the heat-shimmer still rising from her shoulders like she was a banked fire herself. She wasn't looking at him; she was looking at the place where the fire had been, her expression doing something quiet and complicated that he didn't have a word for.

"That's not—" He stopped.

She glanced at him.

"I was going to say that's not a bad thing," he said. "But I don't know if that's true for you, so I stopped."

She looked at him for a moment. Then she smiled — not the big Karlach smile, the one that took up her whole face and made everyone in the vicinity feel like they'd said something clever. This was smaller. Genuine in a different way.

"Thanks for that," she said. "Most people just say it's fine."

"I know," he said. "It's a terrible thing to say."

"It is." She looked back at the fire. "It assumes you know how the person feels about it."

"And you might feel terrible about it."

"I might," she agreed. "I mostly don't. But I might." She rubbed the back of her neck. "It's more just — strange. Like you said. The shape is different." She looked at her hands again. "I keep trying to pick up things I used to like and see if they still fit. Food. Sun. Talking to people." A pause. "They mostly do. Some of them better than I expected. Some of them—" She shook her head. "I had this whole list, in Avernus. All the things I was going to do when I got out. And I'm doing them. I'm — I went to Baldur's Gate, I ate a *ridiculous* amount of food, I watched the sun come up three days in a row—"

"I remember," he said. "You woke me up for it."

"You didn't sleep anyway."

"I was *lying down.*"

"You were absolutely awake." She pointed at him. "You had the eyes."

"I don't know what that means."

"The open eyes. The not-blinking. The 'I am pretending to be asleep but I am doing threat assessment.'"

He opened his mouth. Closed it. "I wasn't doing threat assessment at four in the morning."

"Then what were you doing?"

"I was—" He stopped. He had been, in fact, cataloguing the sounds of the camp. The breathing patterns of everyone in their tents. The rhythm of the night insects. The specific quality of the silence that preceded dawn, which he had learned over 200 years was slightly different from the silence of deep night — a different density, a different texture against the skin. He had been doing this with no particular purpose except that his body had been doing it for 200 years and he hadn't yet taught it to stop.

"Nothing," he said.

Karlach smiled at the fire. "Mm."

---

The moon had moved. He noticed it the way he noticed most things — peripherally, then with full attention when the peripheral registered something changed. It was lower now, the cloud cover thicker, the camp darker for it. The coals were almost out. Just the faintest thread of orange left at the center, more heat than light.

"Are you cold," Karlach said.

"I'm always cold."

"More cold. Than usual."

He considered it. "A little."

She shifted, moving closer along the edge of the fire pit — not close enough to touch, just close enough that the ambient heat coming off her reached him. He felt it on his left arm, the side of his neck, the back of his hand. Like sitting next to a hearth. She didn't say anything about it. She just moved, and the warmth was there, and she looked back at the fire as if nothing had happened.

He looked at his hand in the near-dark. Warm on the left side, cool on the right.

"Thank you," he said.

"Don't mention it."

He wouldn't. He filed it in the category of things that had happened without requiring comment — a category that was, he'd noticed, slowly getting larger. The list of things that could simply be received without being analyzed for motive, without being catalogued as debt or obligation or the opening move in something that would eventually require payment.

Karlach shifted slightly, stretching her legs out toward the cooling coals.

"What do you do," she said, "when you can't lie down anymore."

"I usually read," he said. "Or I walk the perimeter." A pause. "Sometimes I just sit."

"Like now."

"Like now."

"Is it—" She seemed to consider whether to finish. "Does it help? The sitting."

"It passes time," he said. "Which is the closest I've found to help, for most things."

She nodded slowly. "Yeah." She looked up at the sky, tracking the moon the way he had earlier. "In Avernus there wasn't any — time didn't work the same. You'd think it was a day and it'd been a week. Or the other way." She paused. "I kept losing track. Of how long I'd been there. And then I stopped trying to track it because it was making it worse."

"Two hundred years," he said, "and I tracked every one."

She looked at him.

"Not the years themselves," he said. "I lost those. But the nights. The errands. The — every interaction with every person Cazador sent me to." He looked at his hands. "You develop a very precise sense of time when time is the only thing you have any relationship to. When the only question is how many hours until dawn, how many hours until you have to be somewhere, how long you have before—" He stopped.

Karlach was watching him. Not with pity — he checked for it automatically and found none. With attention, that particular quality she had of listening to something as if it were worth listening to.

"Before he needed something from you," she said quietly.

"Yes."

A silence. The last thread of orange in the coals pulsed once and faded to grey.

"I'm sorry," Karlach said. "I know that's not—" She stopped. "I know that doesn't do anything. I just."

"It does something," he said.

She looked at him.

"It does something," he said again. "When someone says it and means it."

Karlach looked at the dead fire for a moment. Then she nodded, once, as if this were information she was filing somewhere careful.

"I'm sorry about Zariel," he said. "And about the engine."

She made a sound — almost a laugh, rough-edged. "Yeah." She rubbed her face with both hands, the gesture of someone very tired. "Yeah, I know. Thanks."

They sat for a moment in the particular silence of two people who have said the most important things and are now letting the air settle around them.

"It's going to be light soon," Karlach said.

He looked east. The sky was still dark but there was something at the horizon — not light yet, just the absence of the deepest dark. The quality of air that preceded dawn, the texture he had learned to recognize.

"Forty minutes," he said. "Maybe fifty."

"Do you—" She paused. "Do you watch it? The sunrise."

"Sometimes."

"Do you want to. Today."

He looked at the horizon. The faint suggestion of change gathering there, unhurried, indifferent to any of it — to Cazador's death or Zariel's legions or the Netherbrain or any of the hundred things that had conspired to leave the two of them sitting here in the cooling dark. The sun came up the same way regardless.

He found, to his mild surprise, that he did.

"Yes," he said.

Karlach settled back on her hands, tilting her face upward. He watched her do it — the deliberate quality of it, the way she positioned herself to receive the light before it even arrived, as if she were making room for it. The engine ticked in her chest, steady and relentless, and she breathed around it with the ease of long practice.

He thought about her list. All the things she'd catalogued in Avernus, held against the possibility of someday. The way she'd come back and found that the things fit, mostly, but she'd changed shape around them. He understood that differently than he had an hour ago — the specific grammar of it, the way you could want something for so long that you arrived at it as a stranger.

He looked east.

The sky was beginning to do something at the horizon. Still dark overhead, but at the edge — a faint, impossible blue, the color of something that hadn't quite decided yet what it was going to be.

"Hey," Karlach said.

He looked at her. She was still looking at the horizon, but she was smiling now — the small one, not the big one.

"We made it," she said. Not triumphant. Just observational. The way you'd note the weather.

He looked at the horizon. The blue was deepening, separating from the black, finding its own particular shade.

"We did," he said.

They sat with that for a while. The camp quiet around them, everyone else still in their tents, the fire cold and the dew on the grass and the sky doing its slow, inevitable work. His hands were warm on the left side. His body was still, for the moment, only cataloguing the light.

He didn't feel lighter. He still didn't feel lighter.

But the sun was coming up, and he was watching it, and nobody had told him to.

That was something. He was fairly certain, sitting there in the cooling dark with Karlach's warmth on his left side and the sky going slowly blue at the edges, that it was something.
