# Inspirations

## Core Influences

### Ocean Vuong (On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous)
- **What we take:** The way he uses small physical details to carry enormous emotional weight. A gesture, a texture, a smell becomes the fulcrum of a scene. His interiority is embodied -- characters think through their bodies, not in abstractions.
- **What we leave:** The lyrical density. Vuong writes prose poetry; we write narrative prose. Our sentences are shorter, more grounded, less inclined toward the associative leaps that make his style so distinctive. We admire the music but keep our own rhythm plainer.
- **Example:** "The scent of a peach is its memory of being a flower." -- This crystallizes the technique: a sensory detail that opens into something psychological without ever stating the psychology directly.

### Kazuo Ishiguro (The Remains of the Day, Never Let Me Go)
- **What we take:** Unreliable interiority through restraint. Ishiguro's characters reveal themselves by what they don't say, what they glide past, what they circle back to without acknowledging why. The reader understands the character better than the character understands themselves. This is central to writing Astarion -- a character whose self-narration is full of deliberate gaps.
- **What we leave:** The glacial pacing. Ishiguro can sustain tension across 300 pages with almost nothing happening; we need to create that tension inside 5,000 words. We compress his technique.
- **Example:** Stevens in Remains of the Day describing his father's tray-carrying as "dignity" -- the reader sees the grief underneath, Stevens does not. That gap is where the story lives.

### Carmen Maria Machado (Her Body and Other Parties)
- **What we take:** The willingness to sit with discomfort without resolving it. Her stories hold tension without rushing to catharsis. Bodies are sites of meaning -- how characters inhabit their physical selves tells you everything about their psychology. Directly relevant to writing a character with Astarion's history of bodily violation.
- **What we leave:** The surrealist elements. Machado breaks realism deliberately; we stay inside it. Her structural experiments are not our tool.
- **Example:** "The Husband Stitch" -- the way the narrator's body is simultaneously the thing she lives in and the thing others claim authority over. That double consciousness maps directly onto Astarion's arc.

### Donna Tartt (The Secret History)
- **What we take:** Atmosphere through accumulated sensory specificity. Tartt builds a world that feels physical -- you can feel the cold, smell the cigarette smoke, hear the specific quality of silence in a room. Her pacing is deliberate without being slow; every scene earns its length.
- **What we leave:** The baroque vocabulary. Tartt writes in a register that's more elevated than ours. We stay at "literary accessible" -- the kind of prose where the craft is visible if you look for it but never gets between the reader and the story.
- **Example:** The opening of The Secret History, where a murder is revealed in the first paragraph and then the novel becomes about understanding why. The technique of giving away the event and making the psychology the mystery is something we use in character studies.

## Fandom Influences

### fourthorseman (AO3)
- **Known for:** Psychologically precise Astarion characterization. Their Astarion thinks in ways that feel true to the game without being limited by it.
- **What we learn from them:** How to write Astarion's deflection patterns -- the humor-as-armor, the way he tests people by being provocative, the moments where the mask slips.
- **Difference from us:** They write in second person, present tense, and lean more heavily into romance as a frame. We use third person, past tense, and center the psychology rather than the relationship.

### spectremoon (AO3)
- **Known for:** Atmospheric, slow-paced BG3 fic with strong prose craft. Their description of the Underdark is some of the best environmental writing in the fandom.
- **What we learn from them:** Sensory worldbuilding that serves character -- the environment reflects and shapes the emotional state of the scene.
- **Difference from us:** They write longer, more plot-driven pieces. We focus on compressed character studies where the "plot" is internal.

## Anti-Influences

### The "and then he realized" internal monologue dump
- **What it looks like:** "Astarion realized then that he had never truly been free. Freedom wasn't just the absence of Cazador's control -- it was the terrifying, beautiful possibility of choosing wrong. He understood, in that moment, that healing wasn't linear."
- **Why we avoid it:** It tells the reader what to think. It resolves ambiguity instead of holding it. It reads as the author explaining the theme rather than the character living it. It is also a strong AI tell -- language models default to this kind of tidy psychological summary.
- **What we do instead:** Show the moment. Show the character's hands, their breathing, the specific small action they take. Let the reader do the work of understanding what it means.

### Purple prose sensory overload
- **What it looks like:** "The moonlight cascaded like liquid silver across the obsidian expanse of his porcelain skin, each contour a testament to centuries of unspoken suffering."
- **Why we avoid it:** It mistakes decoration for depth. Every metaphor should earn its place; stacking them signals insecurity about whether the scene is working. It also flags as AI-generated -- language models love to pile on adjectives.
- **What we do instead:** One precise sensory detail per beat. The moonlight does one specific thing in the scene. The skin is described only if the description tells you something about the character looking at it.

### Fandom-wiki recap narration
- **What it looks like:** Paragraphs that summarize canon events the reader already knows. "Having spent 200 years as Cazador's spawn, Astarion had learned to..." This is the author demonstrating they know the lore rather than inhabiting it.
- **Why we avoid it:** The reader already played the game. They know. Recapping canon signals a writer who doesn't trust the audience. It also eats word count that should go toward the new thing our fic is offering.
- **What we do instead:** Reference canon through specific, oblique details. A flinch at a particular sound. A habit that only makes sense if you know the history. Let canon exist as substrate, not exposition.
